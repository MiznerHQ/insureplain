Switching From Medicare Advantage
Back to Medigap

MEDICARE

What's actually happening

Switching from a Medicare Advantage plan back to Original Medicare with a Medigap policy is possible, but it isn't a single action. It's two separate steps: disenrolling from Medicare Advantage, and applying for and being approved for a Medigap policy.

Most people assume once they leave Medicare Advantage, they can pick up a Medigap plan the way they picked up their original coverage. In most states, that's not how it works. Medigap insurers are generally allowed to ask about your health history and can deny coverage or charge more based on what they find — unless you qualify for a protection called guaranteed issue rights.

That gap between "you can switch" and "you're guaranteed approval" is where most of the confusion — and the risk — lives.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Can you switch from Medicare Advantage back to Medigap without underwriting?
Only if you have guaranteed issue rights. Otherwise, the Medigap insurer can review your health history and may deny you or charge a higher premium.
What happens if you switch to Medigap and get denied?
If you've already left Medicare Advantage, you could be left with only Original Medicare and no supplemental coverage. That's why applying for Medigap before disenrolling matters so much.
Can you switch from Medicare Advantage to Medigap at any time?
No — only during specific windows. These include the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period, the Annual Enrollment Period, or a qualifying Special Enrollment Period.
Why it's not automatic

The switch has two separate approvals

Leaving Medicare Advantage is the easy part — you can do that during an eligible enrollment window with a phone call or a form. Getting approved for a Medigap policy is the part that trips people up, because it depends on your health history and the specific protections you qualify for at the time you apply.

  • Medical underwriting — outside of a protected window, the insurer can review conditions like heart disease, diabetes with complications, COPD, or a recent cancer diagnosis. See how underwriting decisions get made.
  • Guaranteed issue rights — specific situations, like your first 12 months on Medicare Advantage, where the insurer cannot deny you or charge more for health reasons.
  • State-level protections — some states go further than federal rules and offer additional windows where underwriting doesn't apply, so it's worth checking your state's specific rules.
  • Plan-to-plan differences — underwriting standards vary by insurance company, so a denial from one company doesn't necessarily mean denial everywhere.
  • Drug coverage — Medicare Advantage often bundles in Part D. Switching to Medigap means you'll separately need a standalone Part D prescription drug plan.
  • Coverage gaps — timing the switch wrong can leave you with a period of reduced or no supplemental coverage.
Two paths, two outcomes

Guaranteed issue vs. standard underwriting

Which path you're on determines almost everything about how smooth this switch will be.

Guaranteed issue

The insurer must accept you and cannot charge more for health reasons. This applies in specific situations, like switching back within 12 months of first joining Medicare Advantage at 65.

Standard underwriting

The insurer reviews your health history and can approve, approve at a higher rate, or deny coverage. This is the default outside of a protected window.

⚠️ THE ORDER MATTERS

Apply for your Medigap policy and get approved before you disenroll from Medicare Advantage. Doing it in the wrong order is the single most common way people end up with a coverage gap.

How to actually do it

Step-by-step

  1. Confirm your enrollment window. Check whether you're in the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (Jan 1–Mar 31), the Annual Enrollment Period (Oct 15–Dec 7), or a qualifying Special Enrollment Period.
  2. Check whether you have guaranteed issue rights. If you're within your first 12 months on Medicare Advantage, or another qualifying situation applies, underwriting may not apply to you at all.
  3. Apply for your Medigap policy first. Submit your application before disenrolling from Medicare Advantage, and request an effective date that lines up with when your current plan ends.
  4. Wait for approval. If you don't have guaranteed issue rights, the insurer will review your health history before deciding.
  5. Once approved, disenroll from Medicare Advantage. You can do this directly with your plan or by calling 1-800-MEDICARE.
  6. Add a standalone Part D plan. If your Medicare Advantage plan included drug coverage, that ends when you leave it — you'll need to enroll in Part D separately to avoid a coverage or penalty gap.
What to do right now
Before doing anything else, check whether you currently have guaranteed issue rights — it changes your entire strategy. If you're outside a protected window, apply to a Medigap plan first and wait for approval before you touch your Medicare Advantage enrollment. If you're unsure, a licensed agent or your State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP) can walk through your specific situation at no cost.
Frequently asked questions
Can you switch from Medicare Advantage back to Medigap?
Yes, but only during certain enrollment windows, and in most states you'll need to pass medical underwriting unless you qualify for guaranteed issue rights.
Do you need medical underwriting to switch to Medigap?
In most cases, yes. Unless you have guaranteed issue rights, the Medigap insurer can review your health history and may charge more or deny coverage based on certain conditions.
What are guaranteed issue rights?
Guaranteed issue rights are specific situations where a Medigap insurer cannot deny you coverage or charge you more based on health status, such as during your initial Medigap enrollment window or certain trial periods.
When can you switch from Medicare Advantage to Medigap?
You can disenroll from Medicare Advantage during the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (Jan 1–Mar 31), the Annual Enrollment Period (Oct 15–Dec 7), or a qualifying Special Enrollment Period.
What happens if your Medigap application is denied?
If you're denied and you've already left Medicare Advantage, you could be left with only Original Medicare and no supplemental coverage, so applying for Medigap before disenrolling is strongly recommended.
