Does Medigap Require
Medical Underwriting?

Medicare

What's actually happening

Medical underwriting means an insurance company reviews your health history before deciding whether to approve your application, and at what price. For Medigap (Medicare Supplement) plans, whether you go through underwriting depends entirely on timing.

If you apply during a protected window — like your initial Medigap Open Enrollment Period — insurers generally can't use your health history against you. Apply outside that window without another protection in place, and most states allow insurers to ask detailed health questions, charge more, or deny you outright.

This catches a lot of people off guard, especially those who switch from Medicare Advantage back to Medigap years after their initial enrollment window has closed.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Does Medigap require underwriting?
Only if you're outside a protected enrollment window. During those windows, insurers must accept you regardless of health history.
Can you be denied Medigap insurance?
Yes, if you apply outside a protected period. Insurers can legally reject applications based on health conditions when underwriting applies.
Is underwriting the same in every state?
No. Some states, like Connecticut and New York, require guaranteed issue Medigap coverage year-round, regardless of timing.
When underwriting applies

The two paths into a Medigap plan

Every Medigap applicant falls into one of two categories, and which one applies changes everything about your application experience.

  • Guaranteed issue — you have a legal right to buy a Medigap plan without health questions. This applies during your initial 6-month enrollment window, or during specific triggering events. See how pre-existing conditions are typically handled for more on this protection.
  • Medical underwriting — the insurer reviews your health history and can approve, deny, or price your policy based on what they find. This applies any time you're outside a guaranteed issue period.
  • Trigger events for guaranteed issue — losing employer coverage, a Medicare Advantage plan leaving your area, or moving out of your plan's service area can reopen guaranteed issue rights.
  • State variation — a handful of states extend guaranteed issue protections well beyond federal minimums, which changes the underwriting picture significantly depending on where you live.
  • Insurer variation — even within underwriting, standards differ by company. One insurer's questionnaire may flag a condition that another overlooks entirely.
Side by side

Guaranteed issue vs. underwritten

Here's what actually changes depending on which path you're on.

Guaranteed issue

No health questions. Insurer must accept you. Premium can't be raised for health reasons. Available only during specific windows or trigger events.

Underwritten

Detailed health questionnaire required. Insurer can deny coverage entirely. Premium may be higher based on health history and insurer's rating rules.

⚠️ TIMING MATTERS MOST

The single biggest factor in whether you face underwriting isn't your health — it's your timing. Missing a guaranteed issue window is the most common reason people end up underwritten unexpectedly.

How it plays out

What the underwriting process looks like

  1. Submit your application. You'll list your health history and current medications alongside standard enrollment information.
  2. Complete the health questionnaire. Insurers ask about specific conditions — this varies by company, so a condition that disqualifies you with one insurer might not with another.
  3. Insurer reviews your file. They may request medical records or contact your providers to confirm details from your application.
  4. Decision issued. You may be approved at standard rates, approved with a higher premium, or denied outright, depending on the insurer's underwriting guidelines.
  5. If denied, you can reapply elsewhere. Each insurer sets its own standards, so a denial from one company doesn't mean automatic denial everywhere. Check if a missed open enrollment window is part of what's driving the need to apply now.
  6. Compare before committing. If you have any guaranteed issue trigger event coming up — like losing employer coverage — it may be worth timing your application around it.
What to do right now
Check whether you're still inside your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period, or whether a guaranteed issue trigger event applies to your situation. If you're not sure, or if you're weighing Medicare against employer coverage, a licensed insurance professional can confirm your specific rights before you apply.
Frequently asked questions
Does Medigap require medical underwriting?
Yes, in most cases. Outside your Medigap Open Enrollment Period or a guaranteed issue situation, insurers can review your health history and may charge more, deny coverage, or exclude pre-existing conditions.
Can you be denied Medigap insurance?
Yes. If you apply outside your protected enrollment window and don't qualify for guaranteed issue rights, an insurer can legally deny your application based on your health history.
When can you skip Medigap underwriting entirely?
During your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period, which starts the month you're 65 and enrolled in Medicare Part B, or during a guaranteed issue event like losing employer coverage.
What health conditions can affect Medigap underwriting?
Insurers typically ask about conditions like heart disease, cancer, diabetes complications, kidney disease, and recent hospitalizations. Each insurer has its own underwriting questionnaire and standards.
Does medical underwriting affect my Medigap premium?
Yes. Even if you're approved, underwriting can result in a higher premium than someone who enrolled during a protected period, depending on the insurer's pricing rules.
