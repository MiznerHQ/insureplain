What Happens If the Other Driver
Has No Insurance?

Car Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You did everything right. You have insurance. You were driving safely. Then someone hits you — and you find out they have no insurance at all. Now you're stuck with a damaged car, possible injuries, and no one to pay for it.

This happens more than most people realize. Roughly 1 in 8 drivers on the road right now is uninsured. What you do in the next few hours and days determines whether you get paid or get stuck.

Your options when they have no insurance

Where the money can come from

Your Own Policy

If you have uninsured motorist coverage (UM) or collision coverage, your own insurer pays you — then goes after the other driver themselves.

Sue the Driver

You can take the uninsured driver to court. If they have assets or income, a judgment can be enforced. If they have nothing, collecting is difficult.

State Funds

Some states have uninsured motorist guarantee funds. Availability and limits vary significantly by state — not all states have them.

Their Assets Directly

An attorney can help identify wages, bank accounts, or property that can be seized through a court judgment to satisfy your claim.

The coverage that was built for this

Uninsured motorist coverage explained

Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage is the specific protection designed for exactly this situation. If you have it on your policy, your insurer steps in and pays your damages as if they were the at-fault driver's insurer.

  • UMBI (Bodily Injury) — Covers your medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering when the at-fault driver has no insurance.
  • UMPD (Property Damage) — Covers damage to your vehicle when the at-fault driver has no insurance. Some states require a deductible.
  • Underinsured Motorist (UIM) — Similar coverage for when the other driver has insurance but not enough to cover your full damages.
  • Collision coverage — Also pays for vehicle damage regardless of who's at fault. Your deductible applies, but your insurer may recover it from the other driver.

⚠️ Don't have UM coverage?

If you only carry liability insurance (the state minimum), your own policy will not cover your damages when the other driver is uninsured. You'd be limited to suing the driver directly — which can be slow and may result in nothing if they have no assets.

What to do right now — step by step

Your immediate action plan

  1. Call the police. Get an official accident report. This documents that the accident happened and that the other driver was uninsured. Do not skip this step.
  2. Get their information anyway. Name, license plate, driver's license number, and any insurance card they show you — even if it's expired or invalid.
  3. Document everything at the scene. Photos of both vehicles, damage, the road, traffic signs, and any injuries. Get contact info from witnesses.
  4. Call your own insurance company. Report the accident and tell them the other driver is uninsured. Ask specifically about your UM and collision coverage.
  5. Seek medical attention immediately. Even if you feel fine. Injuries from car accidents often appear hours or days later. Having medical records from the day of the accident protects your claim.
  6. Do not accept a cash offer from the other driver. Any amount they offer at the scene is almost certainly far less than your actual damages and medical costs.
  7. Consult a personal injury attorney. Many work on contingency (no upfront cost) and can pursue the other driver's assets if suing makes sense.
Will this raise your rates?

Filing a UM or collision claim after being hit by an uninsured driver typically does not raise your rates — because you were not at fault. Most states prohibit insurers from raising rates on not-at-fault claims. However, policies vary, so ask your insurer directly before filing to confirm how they handle it.

Bottom line: file the claim. The whole point of UM coverage is this exact situation. You paid for it. Use it.
Frequently asked questions
What if I don't have uninsured motorist coverage?
Your collision coverage (if you have it) can still pay for vehicle damage, minus your deductible. For medical bills, your health insurance becomes your primary resource. To recover from the other driver directly, you'd need to sue them — which takes time and may not result in payment if they have no assets.
Can I sue an uninsured driver?
Yes. You can file a lawsuit and obtain a judgment against them. Whether you can actually collect is a separate question. If the driver has no significant assets, income, or property, collecting on that judgment can be very difficult — though wage garnishment is possible in many states.
What if the uninsured driver was also not at fault?
If the accident was your fault, the other driver's lack of insurance doesn't change your liability exposure. Your liability coverage would still handle their damages. The uninsured status matters most when they are the at-fault party and you're trying to recover your losses.
How much does uninsured motorist coverage cost?
UM coverage is typically one of the cheapest add-ons on an auto policy — often $5 to $20 per month depending on your state and coverage limits. Given that 1 in 8 drivers is uninsured, it's widely considered one of the highest-value coverages you can carry.
