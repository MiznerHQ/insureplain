What Is a Deductible —
And Have I Met Mine?

Deductibles
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You went to the doctor, expected your insurance to cover it, and got a bill anyway. Or you're staring at your plan documents trying to figure out why "$2,000 deductible" matters when you're paying a $400 monthly premium too. It feels like you're paying twice.

A deductible is simply the amount you pay out of your own pocket for covered care before your insurance starts paying its share. It's not a fee, a penalty, or a scam — it's a built-in cost-sharing structure, and once you understand how it actually works, your bills stop being a mystery.

The different types you'll run into

Not all deductibles work the same way

  • Individual deductible — The amount one person on the plan must pay before insurance kicks in for their care.
  • Family deductible — A combined total for everyone on the plan. Some plans are "embedded" (each person has their own cap within the family total); others are "aggregate" (the whole family shares one pool).
  • Annual deductible — Resets at the start of each plan year, usually January 1 — though some employer plans reset on a different date.
  • Per-incident deductible — Common in auto and home insurance. You pay it each time you file a claim, not once per year.
  • High-deductible health plan (HDHP) — A plan with a higher deductible in exchange for lower premiums, often paired with an HSA.
How to check if you've met yours

Finding your real number

  1. Log into your insurer's member portal. Most show a running total labeled "deductible met" or "amount applied to deductible" right on the dashboard.
  2. Check your most recent EOB. Every Explanation of Benefits includes a year-to-date deductible summary near the bottom.
  3. Call member services. Ask specifically for your "deductible accumulator status" — this gets you the exact number faster than general questions.
  4. Ask the billing office before your appointment. Front desk staff can usually check in real time and tell you what you'll owe.
  5. Track major expenses yourself. Keep a running list of what you've paid toward covered care — it's the easiest way to estimate before checking officially.
What to do right now
Don't assume every bill applies to your deductible — copays, premiums, and non-covered services usually don't count. Check your member portal first; it's the fastest way to see your real-time balance. If a bill seems higher than expected, call and ask the rep to confirm whether the charge was applied correctly before you pay it.
Frequently asked questions
Does my deductible reset every year?
Yes, for most health plans the deductible resets on your plan year start date — commonly January 1, though employer plans can vary. Whatever you've paid toward it starts back at zero.
Do preventive care visits count toward my deductible?
Usually no. Under the ACA, most preventive services like annual checkups and standard screenings are covered at 100% before your deductible, with no cost to you.
What happens once I meet my deductible?
After you hit your deductible, your plan typically shifts to coinsurance — you and your insurer split the cost of covered care by a percentage — until you reach your out-of-pocket maximum, at which point insurance covers 100%.
