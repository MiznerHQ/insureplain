What Is a Claims Adjuster
and What Do They Do?

Claims
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You filed a claim and now someone called a "claims adjuster" is involved. You might be wondering whose side they're on — yours or the insurance company's.

Here's the straight answer: a claims adjuster works for the insurance company. Their job is to investigate your claim, determine what happened, and decide how much the insurer should pay. Understanding exactly what they do — and what they're looking for — puts you in a much stronger position.

The three types of adjusters

Not all adjusters are the same

Staff Adjuster

A full-time employee of your insurance company. Handles claims in-house. This is the most common type you'll deal with after filing.

Independent Adjuster

A contractor hired by the insurer — often after large storms or disasters when staff adjusters are overwhelmed. Works for the insurer, not you.

Public Adjuster

The only adjuster who works for YOU. You hire them privately to represent your interests and negotiate a higher settlement. They typically charge 10–15% of the final payout.

What they actually do

The adjuster's job, step by step

  1. Review your policy. They read your coverage terms to determine what is and isn't covered before they do anything else.
  2. Investigate the claim. This includes inspecting damage in person, reviewing photos, interviewing you and any witnesses, and requesting police or medical reports.
  3. Assess the damage. For property claims they estimate repair or replacement costs. For auto claims they inspect the vehicle. For liability claims they evaluate injury severity.
  4. Determine fault. Especially important in auto accidents — the adjuster decides who is responsible and to what percentage.
  5. Calculate your payout. Based on your coverage, deductible, and their damage assessment, they put a number on your settlement offer.
  6. Make a settlement offer. They present the offer to you. This is negotiable — the first offer is rarely the final one.
What they're looking for

How adjusters evaluate your claim

  • Policy exclusions — Any reason your coverage may not apply to this specific loss.
  • Pre-existing damage — Damage that existed before your claim event. They will look for it.
  • Inconsistencies in your account — Conflicting statements between your initial report and follow-up conversations.
  • Documentation gaps — Missing receipts, photos, or records that make it harder to verify the loss.
  • Depreciation — Especially for actual cash value policies, they'll reduce your payout based on the age and condition of damaged items.
  • Fraud indicators — Adjusters are trained to flag suspicious patterns. Legitimate claims with good documentation sail through faster.

⚠️ Remember

The adjuster is not your advocate. They're professionally trained to settle claims accurately — which sometimes means paying less than you expect. You have the right to push back on any offer.

How to protect yourself

What to do when an adjuster contacts you

  1. Document everything before they arrive. Take photos and video of all damage immediately after the incident. Timestamp everything.
  2. Don't give a recorded statement without preparation. You're not required to give one on the spot. Ask for time to review your policy first.
  3. Get your own repair estimates. Don't rely solely on the adjuster's assessment. Get 2–3 independent contractor quotes.
  4. Keep a written log. Record every conversation — date, time, the adjuster's name, and what was said.
  5. Never accept the first offer under pressure. You have time to review. A lowball first offer is common practice.
  6. Consider a public adjuster for large claims. For claims over $10,000, a public adjuster's fee often pays for itself in a higher settlement.
What to do right now
If an adjuster has already contacted you, don't panic — and don't rush. Gather your documentation, get independent repair estimates, and write down everything that's been said so far. If the settlement offer feels too low, you can negotiate or request a re-inspection. For large or complex claims, a public adjuster is worth serious consideration. You have rights in this process — use them.
Frequently asked questions
Can I refuse to speak with the insurance adjuster?
You're generally required to cooperate with your insurer's investigation as a condition of your policy — but you can control how and when. You don't have to give a recorded statement immediately. Ask to schedule a call after you've reviewed your policy and gathered your documentation.
How long does an adjuster have to settle my claim?
State laws vary, but most require insurers to acknowledge your claim within 10–15 days and make a decision within 30–45 days. If your adjuster goes silent, follow up in writing and reference your state's fair claims settlement regulations.
What's the difference between an adjuster and an appraiser?
An adjuster handles the overall claim — coverage, liability, and settlement. An appraiser specifically values the damaged property. In disputed claims, both sides can hire their own appraiser and the two appraisers select a neutral umpire to break ties.
Can I negotiate with the claims adjuster?
Yes — and you should. The first settlement offer is often lower than what your claim is worth. Come back with your own documentation: contractor estimates, receipts, comparable replacement costs. A written counteroffer with supporting evidence is far more effective than simply asking for more money.
