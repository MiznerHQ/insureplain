Do You Need Medicare If You
Already Have Employer Insurance?

Medicare

Written by the InsurePlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult your employer's benefits department or a licensed insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You're turning 65, the Medicare paperwork is showing up in your mailbox, and you're still working with solid health insurance through your job. So now you're stuck wondering: do you actually have to deal with this right now, or can it wait?

Here's the plain-English answer: it depends almost entirely on one number — how many employees your company has. That single detail determines whether Medicare becomes your primary coverage automatically, whether you can safely delay it, and whether a penalty is quietly building in the background if you guess wrong.

This isn't a small detail to gloss over. Getting it wrong in either direction can mean unnecessary premiums for coverage you didn't need yet, or a lasting monthly penalty for coverage you delayed too long.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Do I have to enroll in Medicare at 65 if I have employer insurance?
Not necessarily — it depends on your employer's size. Companies with 20 or more employees generally let you delay Part B penalty-free while your group coverage continues. Smaller employers usually don't offer that same flexibility.
Will I be penalized for delaying Medicare while working?
Only if your employer coverage doesn't qualify you for a delay. As long as your coverage is based on current, active employment at a large enough employer, delaying is typically safe. Retiree coverage and COBRA generally don't count.
Should I at least sign up for Medicare Part A even if I keep my job coverage?
Often yes, since Part A is usually premium-free. The main exception is if you're actively contributing to a Health Savings Account, since Part A enrollment stops that immediately.
The real breakdown

What actually determines your answer

A handful of specific factors decide whether you need Medicare now or can safely wait — check each one against your own situation:

  • Employer size is the deciding factor — if your company has 20 or more employees, your group health plan is typically considered primary, and Medicare enrollment can usually wait. Under 20 employees, Medicare generally becomes primary at 65 regardless of your job coverage.
  • "Creditable coverage" status matters — your employer plan needs to be considered at least as comprehensive as Medicare to safely justify delaying enrollment. Confirm this directly with HR rather than assuming.
  • Part A is usually free, so most people take it anyway — since there's typically no premium if you've worked long enough, declining Part A rarely makes sense unless you're actively funding an HSA, since enrollment in Part A ends HSA eligibility immediately.
  • COBRA does not count as active employment coverage — if you leave your job and switch to COBRA, your Medicare Special Enrollment Period clock is generally already running, regardless of how long your COBRA coverage lasts.
  • Retiree health coverage doesn't count either — coverage tied to a former employer, rather than current active work, typically doesn't qualify you to delay Medicare without a penalty.
  • Spousal employer coverage can also qualify — if your coverage comes through a spouse who is still actively working at a large employer, the same delay rules can generally apply to you as well.
The key split

Large employer vs. small employer

This single distinction changes everything about your timeline and what's considered "on time."

20+ employees

Your group plan is typically primary. You can usually delay Medicare Part B penalty-free while actively employed and covered, then get an 8-month Special Enrollment Period once that coverage ends.

Under 20 employees

Medicare generally becomes primary at 65 regardless of job coverage. Delaying enrollment can mean your employer plan pays little or nothing on claims Medicare was expected to cover first.

⚠️ CONFIRM DIRECTLY WITH HR

Employer size thresholds and coordination-of-benefits rules can have exceptions depending on your specific plan and industry. Never assume based on general rules alone — ask your benefits department directly whether your plan is considered primary or secondary to Medicare.

Getting it right

What to check, step by step

  1. Find out exactly how many employees your company has. This single number determines most of what follows.
  2. Ask HR whether your plan is "creditable coverage." Get this confirmation in writing if possible, since you may need it later to avoid a late enrollment penalty.
  3. Decide about Part A separately from Part B. If you're not contributing to an HSA, there's rarely a downside to enrolling in premium-free Part A right away.
  4. Mark your Special Enrollment Period if you plan to delay Part B. Once your active employment or employer coverage ends, you'll generally have 8 months to enroll without penalty — don't let that window close quietly, similar to how missing standard open enrollment can create coverage gaps.
  5. Don't assume COBRA buys you more time. If you're transitioning to COBRA after leaving a job, treat your Medicare enrollment window as already active.
  6. Revisit the decision if your job situation changes. Retirement, a layoff, or a switch to a smaller employer can all change which coverage should be primary.
What to do right now
Find out your employer's exact headcount and confirm with HR whether your health plan is considered creditable coverage relative to Medicare. If you're under 20 employees, don't delay enrollment without professional guidance. If you're 20 or more and planning to keep working, at minimum consider signing up for premium-free Part A unless you're relying on HSA contributions — and if long-term planning is on your mind too, it's worth knowing Medicare generally doesn't cover long-term care the way many people assume.
Frequently asked questions
Do I need to sign up for Medicare if I have employer health insurance?
It depends primarily on the size of your employer. If your employer has 20 or more employees, you can typically delay Medicare Part B without penalty as long as your group coverage continues. If your employer has fewer than 20 employees, Medicare usually becomes your primary coverage at 65 regardless of your job-based plan, and delaying enrollment can leave major gaps.
What is the difference between "creditable coverage" and employer insurance for Medicare purposes?
Creditable coverage means your employer plan is considered at least as good as Medicare's standard benefits, which allows you to delay enrollment without facing a late penalty later. Not all employer plans automatically qualify, so it's worth confirming creditable status directly with your HR or benefits department rather than assuming.
Does COBRA count as coverage that lets me delay Medicare enrollment?
No. COBRA is generally not considered coverage based on current employment, so it does not qualify you for a Special Enrollment Period to delay Medicare without penalty. If you're relying on COBRA after leaving a job, your Medicare enrollment window may already be running.
Can I keep contributing to my HSA if I sign up for Medicare?
No. Once your Medicare Part A coverage begins, you can no longer contribute to a Health Savings Account, even if you're also covered by an HSA-eligible employer plan. This is a common surprise for people who enroll in Medicare Part A automatically while still working and contributing.
What happens if I don't sign up for Medicare Part B during my Special Enrollment Period?
If you miss your 8-month Special Enrollment Period after employer coverage ends, you may have to wait until the next General Enrollment Period to sign up, which can create a coverage gap. You may also face a late enrollment penalty added to your Part B premium for as long as you have Part B.
