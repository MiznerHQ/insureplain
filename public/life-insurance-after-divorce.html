What Happens to Life Insurance
After a Divorce?

Life Insurance & Divorce

Written by the InsurePlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional or divorce attorney about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

Your divorce is finalized, and somewhere in the back of your mind is a nagging question: what happens to that life insurance policy where your ex is still listed as the beneficiary? Nobody brought it up during the settlement, and now you're not sure if it fixed itself automatically or if you're sitting on a serious oversight.

Here's the plain-English truth: a divorce does not automatically rewrite your life insurance policy. Your coverage, and whoever is named on it, generally stays exactly as it was on your wedding day unless you or a court takes action to change it. Whether that's a problem depends heavily on which state you live in, since some states have laws that strip an ex-spouse's beneficiary status automatically, while others leave it entirely up to you.

This single gap — assuming the divorce "handled it" — is one of the most common and costly oversights people make after a split.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Does my ex automatically stop being my beneficiary after divorce?
Only in certain states, and only for certain policy types. Some states have "revocation upon divorce" laws that remove an ex-spouse automatically, while others require you to actively change it yourself. Employer-provided group policies often aren't covered by these state laws at all.
Can I remove my ex-spouse from my policy right now?
It depends on whether they're a revocable or irrevocable beneficiary. A revocable beneficiary can be changed anytime without their permission. An irrevocable beneficiary must give written, notarized consent first.
Do I have to keep my ex as beneficiary if I pay child support or alimony?
Possibly, if it's written into your divorce decree. Courts sometimes require the paying spouse to maintain a policy naming the ex as beneficiary until support obligations end, to guarantee payments continue if something happens to the payer.
The real breakdown

What actually happens to your policy

A few different things can be true at once depending on your policy type and your state, so it's worth checking each of these against your own situation:

  • Your policy stays active as-is — premiums, coverage amount, and beneficiary designation continue unchanged unless someone actively updates them. Divorce alone doesn't touch the contract.
  • State law may or may not step in — roughly half of U.S. states have automatic revocation-upon-divorce statutes for an ex-spouse beneficiary, but the rest do not, and rules vary further depending on the policy type.
  • Employer-sponsored group life insurance is often an exception — many state revocation laws don't reach employer plans, so an ex-spouse could technically remain the named beneficiary unless you change it directly through HR.
  • Court orders can override your preference — if your divorce decree requires coverage for alimony or child support, you may be legally bound to keep a policy active with your ex named, regardless of what you'd otherwise choose. Read our guide on changing a beneficiary for the general process once you're clear to make changes.
  • Cash value may be treated as a marital asset — if you have a permanent policy with cash value built up, some states treat that value as property to be divided in the divorce settlement, separate from the beneficiary question entirely.
  • Joint policies usually need to be split — most insurers don't allow two divorced people to keep sharing one policy, so it typically gets divided into two individual policies, surrendered, or transferred to one spouse.
The key distinction

Revocable vs. irrevocable beneficiary

This one term determines whether changing your beneficiary is quick or requires someone else's sign-off entirely.

Revocable beneficiary

The default for most policies. You can change who's named at any time, for any reason, without needing your ex-spouse's permission or knowledge.

Irrevocable beneficiary

Often set up to secure a financial obligation, like alimony. Removing them requires their written, notarized consent submitted to the insurer — you can't do it unilaterally.

⚠️ CHECK YOUR SPECIFIC STATE

Automatic revocation-upon-divorce laws vary significantly by state, and some have been challenged in court when they conflicted with the original contract terms. Never assume your state's default rule applies to your exact policy — confirm directly with your insurer or attorney.

Getting it sorted

What to check, step by step

  1. Pull up your current policy documents. Confirm who's currently listed as the beneficiary and whether it's designated as revocable or irrevocable.
  2. Read your divorce decree carefully. Look specifically for language about life insurance, alimony, or child support obligations tied to coverage.
  3. Check your state's revocation-upon-divorce rules. A family law attorney can confirm whether your state removes an ex-spouse automatically or not.
  4. Separate employer coverage from personal policies. Group life insurance through work often needs a manual beneficiary update through HR, regardless of what happens with personal policies.
  5. Address any joint policy directly with your insurer. Ask whether it can be split, transferred, or needs to be replaced with new individual coverage. If you're also reassessing whether you can still afford your existing premiums, our guide on what happens if you stop paying covers what's at stake.
  6. Update beneficiaries once you're legally clear to do so. If you're naming a minor child, consider whether a trust or custodian arrangement makes sense, since minors generally can't directly receive a death benefit.
What to do right now
Don't assume your divorce automatically handled your life insurance — check your policy documents and your decree side by side. If your state doesn't automatically revoke an ex-spouse's beneficiary status, or your policy is employer-sponsored, that update is on you to make. If a conversion or rider affects what's changeable on your policy, our guide on insurance riders can help you understand what you're working with before you call your insurer.
Frequently asked questions
Does divorce automatically remove my ex-spouse as my life insurance beneficiary?
It depends entirely on your state. Some states have laws that automatically revoke an ex-spouse's beneficiary status once a divorce is finalized, while other states leave the policy exactly as it was unless you actively change it yourself. Employer-sponsored group life insurance is generally treated differently and is often not covered by these state laws.
What's the difference between a revocable and irrevocable beneficiary?
A revocable beneficiary can be changed by the policyholder at any time without anyone's permission. An irrevocable beneficiary must give written, notarized consent before they can be removed, which sometimes happens when life insurance was used to secure a financial obligation like alimony.
Can my divorce decree require me to keep my ex-spouse as beneficiary?
Yes. If your divorce settlement includes alimony or child support obligations, a court may require you to maintain a life insurance policy with your ex-spouse named as beneficiary until those obligations end. This is sometimes called court-ordered life insurance.
What happens to a joint life insurance policy after divorce?
Joint policies typically need to be split into two individual policies, surrendered, or transferred to one spouse, since most insurers don't allow divorced couples to keep sharing coverage. Whether this is possible depends on your specific insurer and policy terms.
Should I update my life insurance after divorce even if my state removes my ex automatically?
Reviewing your policy is a good idea regardless of your state's default rule, since automatic revocation laws don't always apply to every policy type, and your coverage needs likely changed along with your circumstances. Confirming the details directly with your insurer avoids relying on assumptions.
