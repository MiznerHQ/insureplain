What Is Short-Term
Health Insurance?

Health Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You lost your job, aged off a parent's plan, or missed Open Enrollment — and now you need health coverage fast. Short-term health insurance shows up in your search results looking like a bargain: low premiums, quick approval, coverage that can start tomorrow. It sounds like exactly what you need.

But short-term health insurance is not real health insurance in the way most people assume. It doesn't have to follow ACA rules, which means it can legally exclude your pre-existing conditions, skip entire categories of care, and deny claims for reasons a Marketplace plan never could. The low premium is real. So are the gaps.

Understanding exactly what short-term plans cover — and what they don't — is the difference between a smart bridge solution and an expensive trap that leaves you with massive bills when you actually need care.

Quick answers

What people ask most

What does short-term health insurance cover?
Emergency care, hospitalization, surgery, and basic doctor visits — but not much else. Short-term plans don't have to cover the 10 essential health benefits required by ACA plans. Mental health care, maternity, prescriptions, and preventive care are frequently excluded or severely limited.
How long can you stay on short-term health insurance?
Federally, up to 4 months per plan with renewals allowed up to 3 years total. But states set their own rules — some cap plans at 3 months with no renewal. Always check your state's specific limits before enrolling.
Is short-term health insurance worth it?
Only for healthy people in a short, defined coverage gap. If you have any pre-existing conditions, take regular medications, or might need specialist or maternity care, a short-term plan will almost certainly cost you more in denied claims than you saved on premiums.
Can short-term insurance deny claims for pre-existing conditions?
Yes — this is the biggest risk. Unlike ACA plans, short-term health insurance can deny claims, exclude conditions, or impose waiting periods for anything in your medical history. A condition you've had for years can make an entire category of care uncovered.
Coverage breakdown

What short-term plans cover — and skip

Short-term plans set their own coverage rules. There's no federal requirement to include the benefits that ACA plans must cover. Here's what you typically get and what's typically missing:

✅ Usually Covered

Emergency room visits, inpatient hospitalization, surgery, some outpatient care, and basic doctor visits for new illnesses or injuries that occur after enrollment.

❌ Usually Excluded

Pre-existing conditions, prescription drugs, mental health and substance abuse treatment, maternity care, preventive care, and pediatric services. Check your specific plan — exclusions vary widely.

⚠️ The pre-existing condition trap

Short-term insurers can — and do — deny claims by arguing a condition was pre-existing. This can include conditions you didn't even know you had at enrollment. If you're diagnosed with something after your plan starts but the insurer finds evidence of prior symptoms in your medical records, they may deny the claim entirely. This is legal for short-term plans and is not allowed under ACA rules on pre-existing conditions.

Plan comparison

Short-term vs. ACA Marketplace plans

These are fundamentally different products. Here's how they compare on the things that matter most:

  • Pre-existing conditions — ACA plans must cover them. Short-term plans can exclude them entirely.
  • Essential health benefits — ACA plans must cover all 10 (including mental health, maternity, and prescriptions). Short-term plans cover none of these by default.
  • Premium cost — Short-term plans are typically 50–80% cheaper per month. The savings are real — so are the coverage gaps.
  • Subsidies — ACA plans qualify for income-based subsidies that can dramatically reduce your premium. Short-term plans do not qualify for any subsidy.
  • Enrollment timing — ACA plans require Open Enrollment or a qualifying life event. Short-term plans can be purchased any time of year.
  • Coverage duration — ACA plans run for a full calendar year. Short-term plans run for weeks to months.
  • Renewability — ACA plans renew automatically. Short-term plans can be cancelled by the insurer and often require re-underwriting at renewal.
  • Out-of-pocket maximum — ACA plans have a federally capped out-of-pocket maximum. Short-term plans often have no cap or a much higher one.
Who it's for

When short-term insurance makes sense — and when it doesn't

Short-term health insurance is a tool for a specific situation. Used correctly, it fills a gap. Used incorrectly, it's a financial risk.

Good fit

Healthy adults in a defined, temporary gap — between jobs, waiting for employer coverage to start, or recent college graduates who missed the deadline to enroll elsewhere. No significant medical history, no regular prescriptions.

Bad fit

Anyone with a chronic condition, regular medications, upcoming planned procedures, or a history of significant illness. Also a bad fit for families with children who need pediatric or preventive care, or anyone who might become pregnant.

⚠️ Missing Open Enrollment isn't the end

Before defaulting to a short-term plan, check whether you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period. Job loss, marriage, divorce, having a baby, and moving to a new state all trigger a window to enroll in an ACA plan outside of Open Enrollment. Read our guide on what to do if you missed Open Enrollment before buying a short-term plan.

Before you buy

How to evaluate a short-term plan

  1. Check your state's rules first. Some states — including California, New York, and Massachusetts — ban or severely restrict short-term plans. Others follow the federal maximum of 4 months. Know what's available where you live before comparing plans.
  2. Read the exclusions list in full. Every short-term plan has a list of conditions and services it won't cover. This is not fine print — it's the most important part of the policy. If your condition appears anywhere on that list, assume related claims will be denied.
  3. Check the out-of-pocket maximum. Many short-term plans cap their total payout per year — not your costs, their payout. If a plan has a $1 million benefit limit but no out-of-pocket cap for you, a serious illness could leave you with unlimited bills.
  4. Confirm what counts as a pre-existing condition under that plan. Some plans use a 5-year lookback period. Others go further. Ask specifically how they define and apply pre-existing condition exclusions before you enroll.
  5. Price an ACA plan for comparison. Go to healthcare.gov and enter your income. If you qualify for a subsidy, an ACA plan may cost the same or less than the short-term plan — with far better coverage.
  6. Check the insurer's financial rating. Short-term plans are sold by a wide range of insurers, not all of them well-capitalized. Look for an AM Best rating of A- or better before buying.
What to do right now
If you're considering a short-term plan, first check healthcare.gov to see if you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period or a subsidy that makes an ACA plan affordable — many people are surprised by how low the cost is after subsidies. If a short-term plan is genuinely your best option, read every exclusion before you buy and never assume a condition is covered just because it isn't listed as excluded. If you end up with a denied claim on a short-term plan, read our guide on why insurance denies claims — some short-term denials can still be challenged.
Frequently asked questions
What does short-term health insurance cover?
Short-term health insurance typically covers emergency care, hospitalization, surgery, and some doctor visits. It does not have to cover the 10 essential health benefits required by ACA plans — meaning mental health, maternity care, prescription drugs, and preventive care are often excluded or severely limited. Always read the specific plan's coverage summary before enrolling.
How long can you stay on short-term health insurance?
Federal rules allow short-term plans to last up to 4 months, with renewals up to 3 years total. However, individual states set their own limits — some states cap plans at 3 months and prohibit renewal, while others follow the federal maximum. Always check your state's rules before enrolling.
Is short-term health insurance worth it?
It depends on your situation. Short-term plans can make sense for healthy people in a temporary coverage gap — such as between jobs or waiting for employer coverage to start. They are not a good fit for anyone with pre-existing conditions, regular prescriptions, or who might need maternity, mental health, or specialist care. Always compare the cost of an ACA plan with subsidies before deciding.
Can short-term health insurance deny claims for pre-existing conditions?
Yes. Unlike ACA Marketplace plans, short-term health insurance is not required to cover pre-existing conditions. Insurers can deny claims, exclude conditions entirely, or impose waiting periods for anything in your medical history before enrollment. This is the biggest risk of short-term plans and the most common source of unexpected denied claims.
