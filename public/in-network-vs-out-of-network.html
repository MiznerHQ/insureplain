What Does In-Network vs
Out-of-Network Mean?

Coverage Basics
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
The plain-English answer

In-network means your insurance company has a contract with that doctor, hospital, or lab. They've agreed on discounted rates. You pay less because your insurer and the provider already have a deal.

Out-of-network means no contract exists. Your insurer hasn't agreed on rates with that provider. So you pay full price — or close to it — and your insurance may cover very little or nothing at all.

That's the whole thing. Network = contract = discount. No network = no contract = you pay.

What it actually costs you

The real price difference

In-Network

You pay your normal copay or coinsurance — typically $20–$50 for a visit, or 10–30% of the bill after your deductible. Your insurer pays the rest at the contracted rate.

Out-of-Network

You may pay 40–60% of the bill — or the entire thing. Many plans have a separate, higher out-of-network deductible. Some plans (HMO, EPO) pay $0 out-of-network.

⚠️ The deductible trap

Out-of-network charges usually go toward a separate out-of-network deductible — not your regular one. That means even if you've already met your in-network deductible for the year, you start over at zero for out-of-network costs.

Plan type changes everything

HMO vs PPO vs EPO

  • HMO (Health Maintenance Organization) — Out-of-network coverage is $0 except in emergencies. You must stay in-network. Period.
  • EPO (Exclusive Provider Organization) — Same as HMO for out-of-network: $0. No exceptions outside emergencies.
  • PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) — Covers both in-network and out-of-network, but out-of-network costs significantly more. Most flexible plan type.
  • POS (Point of Service) — Hybrid. You need a referral from your primary care doctor to see specialists, but out-of-network is covered at a higher cost.

Check your insurance card or the summary of benefits to find your plan type before booking any appointment.

Dentist and dental plans

In-network vs out-of-network for dental

Dental insurance works the same way but the numbers are starker. In-network dentists have agreed to accept lower fees. Out-of-network dentists bill whatever they want — your plan may only reimburse up to a set "usual and customary" fee, leaving you responsible for the gap.

Example: Your out-of-network dentist charges $300 for a cleaning. Your plan's usual and customary rate is $180. You pay the $120 difference plus your normal coinsurance on top of that.

📋 Always verify before your appointment

Call your dental insurance and ask: "Is [dentist name] in-network for my plan?" Don't rely on the dentist's office — they sometimes have outdated information about which plans they accept.

If you accidentally went out-of-network

What to do now

  1. Check if the provider is truly out-of-network. Log into your insurance portal and search the provider's NPI number. Sometimes a doctor is in-network even if the hospital they work at is not.
  2. Ask for an in-network exception. If your in-network doctor referred you to an out-of-network specialist, your insurer may be required to apply in-network cost-sharing. This is called a "continuity of care" or "network gap" exception.
  3. Check if surprise billing protections apply. Under the No Surprises Act, you cannot be billed out-of-network rates for emergency care or for out-of-network providers at in-network facilities without your written consent.
  4. Negotiate the bill directly. Out-of-network providers are not bound to your insurer's contracted rates. You can often negotiate a lower amount or request the in-network rate, especially for planned procedures.
  5. File an appeal if your claim was denied. If your insurer denied coverage, appeal in writing with documentation showing why the out-of-network visit was necessary.
What to do right now
Before your next appointment, log into your insurance company's website and use their "Find a Doctor" tool to confirm your provider is in-network. If you're already dealing with an out-of-network bill, call your insurer first and ask about exceptions before you pay anything. If you have an HMO or EPO, staying in-network is not optional — it's the difference between a $30 copay and a $3,000 bill.
Frequently asked questions
Does out-of-network care count toward my deductible?
Sometimes — but usually toward a separate, higher out-of-network deductible, not your regular one. Check your Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) for the exact numbers. Some plans have a combined deductible that applies to both; most don't.
What does "out-of-network cost share" mean?
Cost share is the portion of the bill you're responsible for after insurance pays its part. Out-of-network cost share is almost always higher than in-network — often 40–60% versus 10–30%. Some plans set it at 100%, meaning they pay nothing out-of-network.
Can I be balance billed by an out-of-network provider?
Yes — with limits. Balance billing means the provider bills you for the difference between what your insurer paid and their full charge. The No Surprises Act (effective 2022) bans balance billing in emergencies and for out-of-network providers at in-network facilities, but it doesn't cover all situations. Scheduled out-of-network visits can still result in balance billing.
How do I find out if my doctor is in-network?
Use your insurance company's online provider directory and search by the doctor's name and NPI number. Always call your insurer to confirm — directories can be outdated. Also ask the doctor's office to verify your specific plan, not just the insurance company name.
