What Is a Surprise Medical Bill —
and How Do You Fight It?

Medical Bills
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You went to an in-network hospital. You did everything right. Then a bill arrived from an out-of-network doctor you never chose — an anesthesiologist, an ER physician, an assistant surgeon — and now you're on the hook for thousands of dollars.

That's a surprise medical bill. And since January 2022, federal law — the No Surprises Act — protects you from most of them. Here's what that means for you and exactly what to do if you get one.

What the No Surprises Act covers

Your federal protections

✓ Protected

Emergency care at any facility. Out-of-network providers at in-network facilities (surgeons, anesthesiologists, radiologists). Air ambulance services from in-network companies.

✗ Not covered

Ground ambulance. Situations where you chose an out-of-network provider knowingly and signed a consent form. Services at out-of-network facilities you chose yourself.

If you're protected, the most you can be charged is your in-network cost-sharing amount — your deductible and copay as if the provider were in-network. The insurer and provider handle the rest between themselves.

Why this still happens

Common reasons surprise bills slip through

  • Your insurer processed it incorrectly — The No Surprises Act is still new. Billing errors and insurer mistakes are common. Always check before paying.
  • You signed a waiver without realizing it — Some providers ask you to sign forms that waive your surprise billing protections. These waivers are only legal in limited circumstances.
  • Ground ambulance is excluded — Ground ambulance is one of the biggest gaps in the law. Those bills can still be billed at out-of-network rates.
  • The facility itself was out-of-network — If you chose an out-of-network hospital or clinic, the law may not apply to the facility charges.
  • The bill is legitimate balance billing — In situations not covered by the law, providers can bill you the difference between their charge and what insurance paid.
How to fight back — step by step

Dispute the bill

  1. Don't pay yet. Paying a bill before disputing it can be treated as acceptance. Review the bill in full before sending any money.
  2. Get an itemized bill. Call the provider and request a line-by-line itemized bill. This is your legal right. Review every charge — billing errors are extremely common.
  3. Check your EOB. Pull your Explanation of Benefits from your insurer's portal. Compare what the provider billed vs. what your insurer says you owe. If they don't match, call your insurer first.
  4. Confirm No Surprises Act coverage. Call your insurer and ask directly: "Is this bill covered under the No Surprises Act?" If yes, your insurer must reprocess it at in-network rates.
  5. File a complaint if your insurer won't act. Report violations at federalnosurprises.cms.gov or call 1-800-985-3059. Complaints trigger federal review and often resolve billing disputes quickly.
  6. Negotiate directly with the provider. If the bill isn't covered by the law, call the billing department. Ask for a cash-pay discount, a payment plan, or financial assistance. Most providers have programs — they just don't advertise them.
  7. Ask about financial assistance. Nonprofit hospitals are legally required to have charity care programs. Ask the billing department for a financial assistance application even if you think you don't qualify.
  8. Hire a medical billing advocate. For large bills, a medical billing advocate works on contingency — they only get paid if they save you money. They know every code, every error, and every lever to pull.
The good faith estimate

Your right to a cost estimate upfront

Under the No Surprises Act, if you don't have insurance or you're paying out of pocket, providers must give you a Good Faith Estimate of costs before your appointment. If the final bill exceeds that estimate by more than $400, you have the right to dispute it through a patient-provider dispute resolution process.

⏱ Deadline warning

You have 120 days from the date of the bill to initiate a patient-provider dispute resolution if your final bill exceeds your Good Faith Estimate by more than $400. Don't wait — this window closes fast.

What to do right now
Do not pay the bill yet. Request an itemized statement, pull your EOB, and call your insurer to ask if the No Surprises Act applies. If it does, your insurer must reprocess the claim at in-network rates. If it doesn't, negotiate directly — ask for a cash-pay discount, payment plan, or financial assistance application. Most surprise bills can be reduced significantly. You just have to ask.
Frequently asked questions
What is the No Surprises Act?
The No Surprises Act is a federal law that took effect January 1, 2022. It protects patients from unexpected out-of-network bills for emergency care and for services provided by out-of-network doctors at in-network facilities — like anesthesiologists or radiologists you never chose yourself.
Can I dispute a surprise medical bill after I've already paid it?
It's harder but not impossible. If the bill violated the No Surprises Act, you can still file a complaint with the federal government at federalnosurprises.cms.gov. For billing errors, contact the provider's billing department directly and request a review. Some states also allow clawbacks for overcharges.
What is a surprise medical billing certification form?
This is a form some providers ask you to sign that waives your No Surprises Act protections. Providers can only legally ask you to sign this in very limited situations — for non-emergency services, at least 72 hours before the appointment, and only if an in-network alternative is available. You are never required to sign it for emergency care.
How do I report a surprise billing violation?
File a complaint at federalnosurprises.cms.gov or call 1-800-985-3059. You can also contact your state insurance commissioner, as many states have their own additional surprise billing protections that go beyond the federal law.
