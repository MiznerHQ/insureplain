What Is a Pre-Existing Condition
in Insurance?

Health Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You have a health condition — diabetes, asthma, a past cancer diagnosis, high blood pressure, maybe even a past injury — and you're worried about what that means when you try to get insurance. This question comes up constantly because the rules depend entirely on which type of insurance you're buying and when you're buying it.

A pre-existing condition is any medical condition you had before your new insurance coverage started. It's that simple as a definition — but the consequences vary dramatically depending on your plan type. For most health insurance sold today, pre-existing conditions are fully protected by law. For others, your medical history can still be used against you.

The rules changed significantly after the Affordable Care Act passed in 2010. But those protections don't apply to every type of insurance — and that gap catches a lot of people off guard.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Can insurance deny you for a pre-existing condition?
For ACA Marketplace and employer health plans, no. Insurers cannot deny you coverage or charge you more based on your medical history. However, short-term health plans, life insurance, and long-term disability policies can still factor in pre-existing conditions.
What conditions count as pre-existing?
Any condition you were diagnosed with, treated for, or had symptoms of before your coverage started. The list is broad — diabetes, asthma, heart disease, depression, high blood pressure, cancer, and even past surgeries or injuries. Some insurers outside the ACA have historically flagged pregnancy as a pre-existing condition.
Does a pre-existing condition raise my health insurance premium?
Not on ACA-compliant plans — it's illegal. But on short-term health plans, life insurance, and some supplemental policies, insurers can charge more or exclude your condition from coverage entirely. Know which type of plan you're buying before you assume you're protected.
What is a pre-existing condition waiting period?
A waiting period is a window after coverage starts when your insurer won't pay claims related to a pre-existing condition. ACA and employer group plans cannot impose these. Short-term and some other plan types still can — often 6 to 12 months before they'll cover anything tied to your medical history.
Common examples

What qualifies as a pre-existing condition

Insurers historically cast a wide net when defining pre-existing conditions. Under ACA-protected plans this definition no longer affects your coverage, but it still matters for life insurance, short-term plans, and disability coverage. Here are conditions that have routinely been flagged:

  • Diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2) — One of the most commonly cited pre-existing conditions across all insurance types.
  • Asthma and COPD — Chronic respiratory conditions are flagged across health, life, and disability policies.
  • Heart disease and high blood pressure — Cardiovascular conditions can affect life insurance rates significantly.
  • Cancer (current or history of) — Even a past cancer diagnosis years in remission can affect life and disability coverage.
  • Mental health conditions — Depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, and others are frequently listed.
  • Sleep apnea — Often overlooked but routinely flagged by life and disability insurers.
  • Pregnancy — In ACA plans, pregnancy cannot be treated as a pre-existing condition. Outside ACA plans, historically it has been.
  • Past injuries and surgeries — A knee surgery, back injury, or broken bone on your medical record can surface on non-ACA applications.
  • Obesity — Flagged by life and disability insurers as a risk factor that can affect premiums.
Plan types compared

Where you're protected and where you're not

Your protection depends entirely on which type of insurance you have. This is the single most important thing to understand about pre-existing conditions.

✅ Fully Protected

ACA Marketplace plans and employer group health plans cannot deny coverage, charge higher premiums, or exclude conditions based on your medical history. No waiting periods either.

⚠️ Not Protected

Short-term health plans, life insurance, long-term disability, and supplemental plans can and often do underwrite based on your medical history — charging more, excluding conditions, or denying coverage entirely.

⚠️ Short-term plans are the trap

Short-term health insurance is cheaper than ACA plans — but it doesn't have to follow ACA rules. That means it can exclude your pre-existing conditions from coverage entirely. You might pay months of premiums and then get a claim denied because the condition existed before you enrolled. Always read the exclusions before buying a short-term plan. Learn more in our guide on short-term health insurance.

How to protect yourself

Steps to take if you have a pre-existing condition

  1. Identify what type of plan you're buying. ACA Marketplace and employer group plans protect you fully. Short-term plans, life insurance, and disability policies do not. This single distinction changes everything.
  2. Use the ACA Marketplace during Open Enrollment. Plans sold at healthcare.gov cannot deny you or charge you more based on health history. If you miss Open Enrollment, check if you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period.
  3. Read the exclusions section of any non-ACA plan carefully. Short-term plans list specific conditions they won't cover. If your condition is on that list, any claim related to it will be denied — even if the care seems unrelated.
  4. Apply for life insurance when you're healthy if possible. Life insurers underwrite based on current health. Locking in a policy before a diagnosis typically gets you a better rate class. Post-diagnosis, guaranteed issue policies exist but cost more.
  5. Check if your employer plan has a waiting period for new hires. ACA rules prohibit pre-existing condition waiting periods, but employers can impose a general waiting period (usually 90 days) before any coverage kicks in — not the same thing, but affects timing.
  6. Document your coverage history. If you're moving between employer plans, continuous coverage can protect you from any lookback period arguments under older plan language.
  7. Contact your state insurance commissioner if you believe you've been wrongly denied. ACA protections are enforceable. If an ACA plan is treating a pre-existing condition as a reason to deny claims, that's a violation you can report.
Life and disability insurance

How pre-existing conditions work differently here

The ACA protections that cover health insurance don't apply to life insurance or disability insurance. These products are still fully underwritten — meaning the insurer reviews your medical history and decides what to charge or whether to cover you at all.

Life Insurance

Insurers can deny coverage, charge higher premiums, or add exclusion riders based on pre-existing conditions. A diabetes diagnosis might move you from a "Preferred" rate to a "Standard" or "Substandard" rate — sometimes doubling your premium.

Disability Insurance

Insurers can exclude specific body parts or conditions from coverage. If you have a pre-existing back condition, your disability policy might include an exclusion rider that won't pay if you become disabled due to your back — even if the injury happened after you enrolled.

⚠️ Guaranteed issue vs. medically underwritten

Some life insurance products are "guaranteed issue" — no medical questions, no exam, no denial. These are typically small face-value policies targeted at older adults. The tradeoff: premiums are much higher and there's often a graded death benefit that limits the payout in the first 2–3 years of the policy.

What to do right now
If you're shopping for health insurance and have a pre-existing condition, your safest move is an ACA Marketplace plan — your condition cannot be used against you by law. If you're looking at a short-term plan because of the lower premium, read the exclusions list first and assume anything related to your condition will be denied. For life or disability insurance, apply sooner rather than later — the younger and healthier you are at the time of application, the better your rate class. If you've already had a claim denied due to a pre-existing condition on an ACA plan, read our guide on why insurance denies claims and file an appeal.
Frequently asked questions
Can insurance companies deny coverage for pre-existing conditions?
For health insurance sold on the ACA Marketplace or through employers, no — insurers cannot deny you coverage or charge you more because of a pre-existing condition. However, short-term health plans, life insurance, and some disability policies can still factor in your medical history and deny coverage or charge higher rates.
What counts as a pre-existing condition?
Any medical condition you were diagnosed with, treated for, or showed symptoms of before your insurance coverage started. Common examples include diabetes, asthma, cancer, heart disease, high blood pressure, depression, and even pregnancy in some non-ACA plans. The list insurers use outside the ACA can be surprisingly broad.
Does a pre-existing condition affect my premiums?
Under ACA-compliant health plans, pre-existing conditions cannot affect your premium — it's prohibited by law. But for short-term health plans, life insurance, and long-term disability insurance, insurers can and often do charge higher premiums or exclude certain conditions from coverage entirely. Always confirm which type of plan you're buying.
What is a pre-existing condition waiting period?
A waiting period is a set amount of time after your coverage starts during which your insurer will not pay claims related to a pre-existing condition. ACA Marketplace and employer group plans cannot impose waiting periods for pre-existing conditions, but short-term and some other plan types still can — sometimes 6 to 12 months before they'll cover anything tied to your medical history.
