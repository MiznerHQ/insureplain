Liability vs Full Coverage
Car Insurance: What's the Difference?

Auto Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You're shopping for car insurance and you see two options: liability only or full coverage. The price difference is significant and you're trying to figure out if full coverage is actually worth it — or if you're just paying for coverage you'll never use.

Here's the plain truth: "full coverage" isn't a real insurance term. It's a shorthand that means you have liability plus collision plus comprehensive. Understanding what each piece does is how you make the right call for your situation.

Side by side — what each one covers

Liability vs full coverage

Liability Only

Covers damage you cause to other people's cars and property, and their medical bills. Does NOT cover your own car. Required by law in almost every state.

Full Coverage

Everything liability covers, PLUS damage to your own car from accidents (collision) and non-accident events like theft, weather, and animals (comprehensive).

⚠️ The gap most people miss

If you only have liability and you cause an accident, the other driver's car is covered — but yours is not. You pay out of pocket to fix or replace your own vehicle.

What full coverage actually includes

Breaking down the three parts

  • Liability — Pays for damage and injuries you cause to others. Split into bodily injury liability and property damage liability. Every driver needs this at minimum.
  • Collision — Pays to repair or replace your car after an accident, regardless of who's at fault. Covers hitting another car, a guardrail, or a pothole.
  • Comprehensive — Pays for damage to your car from non-collision events: theft, vandalism, hail, flooding, fire, hitting a deer, falling objects.
  • Uninsured/Underinsured motorist — Often bundled with full coverage. Protects you if the at-fault driver has no insurance or not enough to cover your damages.
  • Medical payments / PIP — Covers your medical bills after an accident regardless of fault. Required in no-fault states, optional elsewhere.
The cost difference

What does full coverage actually cost more?

The national average difference between liability-only and full coverage is roughly $100–$150 per month depending on your car, location, driving history, and deductible. Here's how to think about whether that's worth it:

Full Coverage May Not Be Worth It If...

Your car is worth less than $4,000–$5,000. At that point, annual premiums plus your deductible may exceed what the insurer would actually pay out if the car were totaled.

Full Coverage Is Worth It If...

Your car is worth $8,000 or more, you couldn't afford to replace it out of pocket, or your lender requires it. The math strongly favors full coverage on newer or financed vehicles.

Do I need full coverage?

When full coverage is required vs optional

  • Financed car — Your lender almost certainly requires full coverage for the life of the loan. Dropping to liability only while you have a car loan is a contract violation and could result in the lender force-placing expensive coverage on your behalf.
  • Leased car — Lease agreements universally require full coverage, often with specific minimum limits higher than state requirements.
  • Paid-off car, newer model — Full coverage is strongly recommended. A single accident or theft could cost you tens of thousands of dollars.
  • Paid-off car, older low-value model — This is where liability-only makes financial sense for many drivers. Run the numbers: if your car is worth $3,000 and your deductible is $1,000, the most you'd ever collect is $2,000.
  • You live in a high-theft or severe weather area — Comprehensive coverage pays off faster in areas with high vehicle theft rates or frequent hail, flooding, or hurricane risk.
How to decide — step by step

The right way to choose

  1. Check if you have a loan or lease. If yes, full coverage is not optional — it's required. Stop here and make sure you have it.
  2. Look up your car's current market value. Use Kelley Blue Book or Edmunds. Get the private party value for your year, make, model, and mileage.
  3. Find out what full coverage costs you annually. Call your insurer or get a quote online. Get the number for your specific deductible ($500 or $1,000).
  4. Do the math. If your car is worth $5,000 and full coverage costs $1,200/year with a $1,000 deductible, the max you'd ever collect is $4,000. You'd break even in about 3–4 years of never filing a claim.
  5. Consider your financial cushion. Could you pay $8,000 out of pocket to replace your car tomorrow if it were totaled? If no, full coverage is worth it regardless of age.
  6. Raise your deductible to lower your premium. If you want full coverage but it feels expensive, raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 can cut your premium meaningfully — as long as you can afford the deductible if you need to file.
What to do right now
If you have a car loan or lease, confirm you have full coverage today — not tomorrow. If your car is paid off, look up its current market value and compare it to what full coverage costs you annually. The break-even math usually takes about 5 minutes and gives you a clear answer. When in doubt, keep full coverage — the one time you need it and don't have it is the most expensive mistake you can make.
Frequently asked questions
Is full coverage required by law?
No — liability insurance is required by law in almost every state, but full coverage is not a legal requirement. It becomes required when your lender or lessor mandates it as a condition of your loan or lease agreement.
What happens if I drop to liability only while I still have a car loan?
Your lender will find out — they're listed on your policy and get notified of coverage changes. They can force-place an insurance policy on your vehicle, which is typically far more expensive than what you'd pay on your own, and add it to your loan balance. Don't do this.
Does full coverage cover a rental car if mine is in the shop?
Not automatically. Rental reimbursement is a separate add-on that costs a few dollars a month. Check your policy — many people assume it's included in full coverage and discover it isn't after an accident.
Can I have full coverage on one car and liability only on another?
Yes. You can carry different coverage levels on different vehicles on the same policy. This is common when one car is newer or financed and another is an older paid-off vehicle you use less frequently.
