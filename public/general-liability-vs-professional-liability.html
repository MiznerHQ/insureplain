General Liability vs Professional Liability Insurance
What's the difference, and which one do you need?

Business Insurance

Written by the InsurePlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You're starting a business — or you already have one — and someone mentioned you need "liability insurance." Then you look it up and see two different types: general liability and professional liability. They sound similar but cover completely different risks. And now you're stuck wondering: Do I need both? Which one actually matters for what I do? Am I leaving myself exposed if I pick the wrong one?

Here's the plain-English version: General liability covers physical risks — someone slips in your office, you break something at a client's site, or you accidentally damage property. Professional liability covers mistakes in your work — bad advice, missed deadlines, errors in a deliverable, or negligence claims about the quality of your services. They are not interchangeable, and many businesses need both.

This guide walks through exactly what each policy covers, how they differ, and how to figure out which one applies to you — without the insurance-speak.

Quick answers

What people ask most

What does general liability insurance actually cover?
Bodily injury, property damage, and personal injury claims. If a customer trips and falls in your office, or you accidentally damage a client's equipment on a job site, general liability pays for the resulting medical bills or repair costs.
What does professional liability insurance actually cover?
Negligence, errors, and omissions in your professional services. If a client sues you because a project failed, your advice was wrong, or you missed a critical deadline, professional liability (also called E&O) covers the legal costs and any settlement or judgment.
Can I just get one policy instead of both?
No — they cover completely different risks. You can sometimes bundle them with the same insurer, but a general liability policy will not pay for professional mistakes, and a professional liability policy will not pay for a customer's broken wrist.
Do freelancers need professional liability insurance?
Often, yes — depending on your work. If you provide advice, design work, consulting, writing, coding, or any professional service that a client could claim was done wrong, professional liability protects you from the financial fallout of that claim.
Coverage breakdown

What general liability insurance covers

General liability insurance — sometimes called commercial general liability (CGL) — protects your business against the everyday physical risks of running a business. It's the policy that kicks in when something physical goes wrong.

  • Bodily injury — a client slips on a wet floor in your lobby and breaks their wrist. General liability pays their medical bills and any legal costs if they sue.
  • Property damage — you're at a client's office and accidentally knock over an expensive monitor. General liability pays to replace it.
  • Personal injury — you get sued for libel, slander, or false advertising. General liability covers that, even if it's not physical.
  • Advertising injury — your marketing materials accidentally infringe on someone else's copyright. General liability covers the defense and settlement.
  • Products and completed operations — you sell a product that injures someone, or your completed work causes damage later. General liability covers it, with some time limits.

Most general liability policies are occurrence-based, meaning they cover incidents that happen during the policy period — even if the claim is filed years later. That's an important distinction from professional liability, which typically uses a different trigger.

Coverage breakdown

What professional liability insurance covers

Professional liability insurance — also called errors and omissions (E&O) insurance — protects you when someone claims your professional work caused them financial harm. It's about the quality of your services, not the physical safety of people or property.

  • Negligence — a client claims you failed to meet the standard of care for your profession and it cost them money. Professional liability covers your defense and any settlement.
  • Errors and omissions — you made a mistake in a contract, a financial report, or a design, and the client lost money as a result. Professional liability pays for the claim.
  • Missed deadlines — you delivered work late and the client says it cost them a deal. Professional liability covers that, depending on the policy wording.
  • Incomplete work — the client claims your work was incomplete or not what they paid for. Professional liability covers the dispute.
  • Breach of contract — if the contract dispute involves your professional services, professional liability often covers the legal costs (though not the contract itself).

Professional liability policies are typically claims-made, meaning they only cover claims filed during the policy period. That's different from general liability's occurrence-based structure, and it matters if you switch policies — more on that below.

⚠️ Claims-made vs occurrence matters

General liability uses occurrence-based coverage — the policy in effect when the incident happened pays the claim, even if the claim is filed years later. Professional liability uses claims-made coverage — the policy in effect when the claim is filed pays, which means you need to buy "tail coverage" when you switch insurers to stay protected for past work. Always ask your agent about this when you're buying or changing a professional liability policy. For a broader look at what triggers coverage and when, see our guide on how claims handling works.

Side by side

General liability vs professional liability — at a glance

Here's the clearest way to separate them: ask yourself what the claim is about. If it's about a person or property that got physically harmed, that's general liability. If it's about the quality of the work you delivered, that's professional liability.

General liability

Covers: Bodily injury, property damage, personal injury (libel/slander), advertising injury.

Who needs it: Any business with a physical location, clients who visit you, or work done on others' property.

Trigger: Occurrence-based — covers incidents during the policy period.

Professional liability

Covers: Negligence, errors, omissions, missed deadlines, incomplete work, professional mistakes.

Who needs it: Any business that gives advice, provides professional services, or delivers work that can be "wrong" in a client's view.

Trigger: Claims-made — covers claims filed during the policy period.

⚠️ The big exclusion to watch

General liability does not cover professional mistakes. If you give bad advice and the client loses $50,000, your general liability policy will not pay a cent. That's exclusively professional liability territory. And professional liability does not cover physical injuries or property damage — those are general liability claims. Who needs what

Do you need one or both?

The answer depends on your business. Here's a decision framework that covers most scenarios.

You almost certainly need general liability if:

  • You have a physical office — customers, vendors, or delivery people visit your space.
  • You work at client sites — you're in other people's offices, homes, or job sites.
  • You sell products — anything that could injure someone or damage property.
  • You lease space — many commercial leases require you to carry general liability.

You almost certainly need professional liability if:

  • You give advice — financial, legal, medical, management, marketing, or any other professional guidance.
  • You produce work products — software, designs, reports, contracts, or anything a client relies on.
  • You have a professional license — most states require proof of professional liability (or E&O) for licensed professionals.
  • Your contract requires it — many client contracts include a professional liability insurance requirement.

Many businesses need both. A consultant who meets clients in their office needs general liability for the physical space and professional liability for the advice they give. A contractor who builds websites needs general liability for on-site meetings and professional liability for code that breaks a client's site. A real estate agent needs general liability for the open house and professional liability for errors in a contract.

If you're still unsure, talk to a commercial insurance agent — they'll walk through your specific business activities and tell you what you actually need. What to do right now
Step 1: List out your business activities. Do you have a physical location? Do clients visit you or do you visit them? Do you give advice or produce work?

Step 2: Check any contracts you've signed — many client agreements require specific types of liability coverage.

Step 3: Call a commercial insurance agent (or use a broker like Hiscox, The Hartford, or a local independent agent) and describe exactly what you do. They'll tell you which policies apply and what limits make sense for your situation.

Step 4: If you're currently operating without liability insurance and you're not sure which you need, getting at least general liability in place is usually the faster, cheaper first move — but don't ignore professional liability if you provide services that can be "wrong." Frequently asked questions
What is the difference between general liability and professional liability insurance?
General liability covers physical risks like customer injuries or property damage. Professional liability covers financial harm from mistakes, errors, or negligence in the services you provide — things like bad advice, missed deadlines, or incomplete work.
Do I need both general liability and professional liability insurance?
It depends on your business. Many businesses need both because each covers different types of risk. A consultant who meets clients in their office needs general liability for the physical space and professional liability for the advice they give.
Is professional liability the same as errors and omissions (E&O) insurance?
Yes — professional liability insurance and errors and omissions insurance are the same thing. E&O is simply the term most commonly used in certain industries, while professional liability is the broader category name.
Does general liability cover lawsuits?
Yes, general liability covers lawsuits arising from bodily injury, property damage, or personal injury (like defamation or slander) that happen on your premises or as a result of your business operations — but it does not cover claims about the quality of your professional work.
How much does professional liability insurance cost?
Premiums typically range from $500 to $3,000 per year depending on your profession, claims history, and coverage limits. High-risk fields like healthcare or legal services are on the higher end; lower-risk consulting or writing roles are on the lower end.
