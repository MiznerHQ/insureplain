Why Did My Car Insurance
Claim Get Denied?

Auto Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You were in an accident, filed a claim, and expected your insurance to handle it. Instead you got a denial letter. It feels like a betrayal — you've been paying premiums and now they won't pay out when you actually need them.

Here's what you need to know: a denial is not always final. Car insurance companies deny claims for specific documented reasons — and many of those reasons can be challenged, corrected, or appealed. Understanding exactly why your claim was denied is the first step to getting it paid.

The most common denial reasons

Why car insurance claims get denied

  • Policy lapsed due to non-payment — If your premium payment was late and your policy had lapsed at the time of the accident, the claim will be denied. This is the most common and hardest to fight.
  • The damage isn't covered by your policy — Collision damage requires collision coverage. Flood damage requires comprehensive. If you only have liability, your own car damage won't be covered.
  • You were excluded from the policy — If the listed driver who had the accident is excluded from the policy by name, the claim is denied regardless of coverage.
  • The accident was caused by an excluded activity — Using a personal vehicle for rideshare, delivery, or business without a commercial endorsement can void coverage.
  • Misrepresentation on your application — If the insurer finds that information you provided when buying the policy was inaccurate — your address, driving history, or primary driver — they can deny the claim.
  • You filed too late — Most policies require you to report an accident promptly. Waiting weeks or months can result in automatic denial.
  • The damage was pre-existing — If the adjuster determines the damage existed before the reported incident, the claim will be denied.
  • Intentional damage — Insurance does not cover damage you caused intentionally, including staged accidents.
Misrepresentation — the one to watch

What "denied for misrepresentation" means

This is one of the most searched denial reasons — and one of the most alarming to receive. Misrepresentation means your insurer believes information on your policy application was inaccurate.

It does not always mean you lied intentionally. Common innocent examples include listing the wrong primary driver, using your old address when you moved, or not disclosing a teenage driver in the household.

⚠️ This one has legal consequences

A misrepresentation denial can lead to policy cancellation and make it harder to get coverage elsewhere. If you receive this denial reason, consult an insurance attorney before responding — not after.

How to fight back — step by step

What to do when your claim is denied

  1. Read the denial letter word for word. Identify the exact reason cited and the specific policy language they're referencing. This is the foundation of your appeal.
  2. Call your insurance company. Ask for a detailed verbal explanation. Get the representative's name, employee ID, and document the date and time of the call.
  3. Pull your full policy document. Read the exclusions and coverage sections yourself. Sometimes adjusters misapply policy language — and catching that is how claims get overturned.
  4. Gather all your evidence. Photos from the scene, police report, witness statements, repair estimates, and any communication with the other driver.
  5. File a formal written appeal. Send it via certified mail with return receipt. Include all supporting documentation and reference the specific policy language you believe supports coverage.
  6. File a complaint with your state insurance commissioner. This is free and often produces faster results than the internal appeal process alone. Insurers take commissioner complaints seriously.
  7. Consult a public adjuster or insurance attorney. For large claims, a public adjuster works on your behalf — not the insurer's. They typically charge a percentage of the final settlement but can significantly increase payouts.
The other driver's insurance denied you too

When the other driver's insurer denies your claim

If the other driver was at fault but their insurance is denying your claim, you have additional options your own insurer may not have told you about.

Use Your Own Coverage

File under your collision coverage and let your insurer pursue the other driver's insurer through subrogation. You pay your deductible upfront but get it back if they recover.

File a Complaint

The other driver's insurer still has obligations under state law. File a complaint with your state insurance commissioner — they can investigate bad faith claim handling.

What to do right now
Don't assume the denial is final and don't pay out of pocket before you've appealed. Find the exact denial reason in your letter, call your insurer to confirm it, and pull your policy document to read the relevant exclusion yourself. File your appeal in writing via certified mail. If the denial involves misrepresentation or a large dollar amount, get an attorney involved before you respond — your words during the appeal process can be used against you later.
Frequently asked questions
Can my insurance deny a claim if the accident wasn't my fault?
Yes — your own insurer can deny your claim regardless of fault if your policy doesn't cover the type of damage, your policy lapsed, or an exclusion applies. Fault determines which insurer pays, but it doesn't override policy exclusions.
How long do I have to appeal a denied car insurance claim?
It varies by insurer and state, but most give you 30–180 days from the denial date to file a formal appeal. Your denial letter should state the deadline. Missing it typically forfeits your right to contest — act fast.
What is bad faith insurance and does it apply to my situation?
Bad faith occurs when an insurer denies a valid claim without a reasonable basis, delays payment unreasonably, or fails to investigate properly. If you believe your claim was denied in bad faith, you may have grounds for a lawsuit beyond just the claim amount — including damages and attorney fees in some states.
Will a denied claim affect my insurance rate?
The denial itself typically doesn't raise your rate — but the underlying incident (accident, damage report) may already be on your record. Filing a claim that gets denied can still show up in claims history databases like CLUE, which insurers check when you apply for new coverage.
