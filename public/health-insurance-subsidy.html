What Is a Health Insurance Subsidy
and Do I Qualify?

ACA / Marketplace
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Income limits and subsidy amounts change annually. Always verify current figures at healthcare.gov or with a licensed insurance professional.
What's actually happening

A health insurance subsidy is money the federal government pays toward your monthly health insurance premium so you pay less. You don't see the cash — it goes directly to your insurance company on your behalf. Your bill is just lower every month.

The official name is the Premium Tax Credit (PTC). It's part of the Affordable Care Act and is only available for plans purchased through the official marketplace at healthcare.gov. If your employer offers coverage or you're on Medicaid, you generally don't qualify.

The 2026 income limits

How much can I earn and still qualify?

Eligibility is based on your household income as a percentage of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). For 2026, you generally qualify if your income falls between 100% and 400% of the FPL — though expanded rules may push that ceiling higher depending on current law.

⚠️ The subsidy cliff

If your income goes even $1 over 400% FPL, you could lose the entire subsidy under standard rules. This is called the subsidy cliff. If you're close to the limit, talk to a navigator or broker before the end of the year — there may be ways to manage your reported income.

The qualification checklist

Do I qualify?

You need to meet all of these to be eligible:

  • Income in range — Your household income is between 100% and 400% of the Federal Poverty Level (or higher if expanded subsidies apply).
  • No affordable employer coverage — Your employer either doesn't offer insurance, or the coverage they offer costs more than ~9.02% of your household income for the employee-only plan.
  • Not on Medicare or Medicaid — If you qualify for either program, you're ineligible for marketplace subsidies.
  • Enrolled through the marketplace — You must buy your plan through healthcare.gov (or your state's exchange). Off-exchange plans don't qualify.
  • U.S. citizen or lawfully present immigrant — Undocumented immigrants are not eligible.
  • File a federal tax return — You must file taxes for the year you receive the subsidy, even if you normally wouldn't have to.
  • Not incarcerated — People currently in prison do not qualify.
Two types of subsidies

Premium Tax Credit vs Cost-Sharing Reductions

Premium Tax Credit

Lowers your monthly premium. Available to anyone between 100–400%+ FPL who qualifies. Applied automatically when you enroll — you never see the money.

Cost-Sharing Reduction

Lowers your deductible, copays, and out-of-pocket max. Only available if your income is below 250% FPL and you pick a Silver plan specifically.

If your income is below 250% FPL, always compare Silver plans first — the cost-sharing reduction can be worth more than the premium savings from a cheaper Bronze plan.

How to actually get it

Step-by-step

  1. Go to healthcare.gov (or your state's marketplace if your state runs its own). Create an account.
  2. Enter your household information — size, location, income estimate for the coming year.
  3. The site calculates your subsidy automatically. You'll see plans with your estimated credit already applied to the price.
  4. Choose a plan and enroll. The subsidy goes directly to the insurer — your monthly bill reflects the reduced amount.
  5. Reconcile at tax time. When you file your federal taxes, you'll complete Form 8962. If you underestimated income, you may owe some back. If you overestimated, you get a refund.

📅 Open enrollment timing

The standard marketplace open enrollment window runs November 1 through January 15 each year. Outside that window, you need a qualifying life event (job loss, marriage, birth of a child, etc.) to enroll. Missing open enrollment means waiting a full year.

What to do right now
Go to healthcare.gov and use the quick screener — it takes about 3 minutes and tells you immediately if you're likely eligible and how much your credit might be. You don't have to enroll to see your estimate. If you're close to the income limits, report your best estimate of this year's income — you can update it anytime during the year if your situation changes.
Frequently asked questions
What if my income changes during the year?
Report it immediately at healthcare.gov. Your subsidy is based on your projected annual income. If you earn more than expected and don't update it, you'll owe the difference back when you file taxes. If you earn less, you'll get a bigger refund.
Can I get a subsidy if I'm self-employed?
Yes — self-employed people are among the most common subsidy recipients. Your income for subsidy purposes is your net self-employment income after business deductions, not your gross revenue.
What is the subsidy cliff and how do I avoid it?
The subsidy cliff is the income threshold where a small increase in earnings causes you to lose eligibility for the full premium tax credit. Contributing to a traditional IRA, HSA, or SEP-IRA can reduce your modified adjusted gross income and keep you under the limit. A tax professional can help you plan this.
Do subsidies apply to dental or vision plans?
No. The Premium Tax Credit only applies to qualifying health plans — not standalone dental or vision coverage sold through the marketplace.
