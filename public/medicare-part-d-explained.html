Medicare Part D,

What's actually happening

Medicare Part D is the piece of Medicare that pays for prescription drugs you pick up at a pharmacy. Original Medicare (Parts A and B) doesn't cover most outpatient prescriptions on its own — Part D fills that gap.

Part D isn't run by the government directly. It's sold through private insurance companies that Medicare approves and regulates, either as a standalone drug plan (PDP) you pair with Original Medicare, or bundled into a Medicare Advantage plan (MA-PD).

Every Part D plan has its own list of covered drugs, called a formulary, and its own pricing. Two people on "Medicare Part D" can pay very different amounts depending on which plan they picked and which medications they take.

Quick answers

What people ask most

IS MEDICARE PART D MANDATORY?
Not legally required, but skipping it usually costs you later. If you don't have Part D or other creditable drug coverage and go 63+ days without it, you'll likely face a permanent late enrollment penalty if you sign up later.
WHAT'S THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN PART D AND MEDICARE ADVANTAGE DRUG COVERAGE?
Where the drug benefit lives, not what it covers. A standalone Part D plan pairs with Original Medicare. Many Medicare Advantage plans (MA-PD) build drug coverage directly into the same plan as your medical benefits, often for less.
CAN I SWITCH PART D PLANS ANYTIME?
Generally no — you're locked in outside of specific windows. The main window is the Annual Enrollment Period, October 15 through December 7 each year, with a few exceptions for qualifying life events.
The money side

What Part D actually costs in 2026

Part D pricing has several separate pieces, and it helps to know what each one is doing:

  • Monthly premium — the average standalone Part D premium in 2026 is around $34.50, though plans range from $0 to over $100 depending on coverage generosity.
  • Deductible — the most any plan can charge is $615 in 2026. Plenty of plans set it lower or waive it for certain drug tiers, especially generics.
  • Coinsurance/copays — after the deductible, you typically pay a percentage or flat copay per prescription, based on which tier your drug falls into on the plan's formulary.
  • Annual out-of-pocket cap — once you've spent $2,100 out-of-pocket on covered drugs in 2026, your plan pays 100% of covered drug costs for the rest of the calendar year.
  • IRMAA surcharge — higher earners pay an extra monthly amount on top of their plan's premium, ranging from about $14.50 to $91 in 2026 depending on income.
What changed

The donut hole is gone — here's what replaced it

For years, Part D had a notorious "coverage gap" — the donut hole — where costs spiked in the middle of the year. Starting in 2025, the Inflation Reduction Act eliminated it entirely. There's now a hard annual cap instead.

STANDALONE PDP

Pairs with Original Medicare. Average premium around $34.50/month in 2026. You choose it separately from your medical coverage.

MA-PD (BUNDLED)

Built into a Medicare Advantage plan. Often $0 added premium, since insurers use rebate dollars to subsidize the drug benefit.

⚠️ ONE COVERAGE, TWO PATHS

You can't have both a standalone PDP and separate MA-PD drug coverage at once — enrolling in one to a Medicare Advantage plan that includes drug coverage typically drops you from a standalone plan automatically.

Choosing a plan

How to pick a Part D plan without guessing

  1. List your current medications. Include dosage and how often you refill — this is what actually drives your real-world cost, not the sticker premium.
  2. Check the formulary tier for each drug. The same medication can sit on a cheap tier with one plan and an expensive one with another.
  3. Watch for prior authorization flags. Some drugs, including many GLP-1 medications, require your doctor to justify medical necessity before the plan will pay — see what happens when a prior authorization gets denied.
  4. Confirm your pharmacy is in-network. Out-of-network pharmacies can mean paying full price even with coverage.
  5. Compare total annual cost, not just premium. A $0 premium plan with a high deductible can cost more overall than a $40 premium plan with better drug tier pricing.
  6. Check if you already have creditable coverage. If you're still working and covered through an employer, confirm whether that plan counts as creditable before deciding whether you even need Part D right now.
What to do right now
Pull up your current medication list and check it against a couple of plan formularies side by side before Annual Enrollment closes. If you're new to Medicare, don't let your Initial Enrollment Period lapse without at least confirming you have creditable drug coverage somewhere — the penalty for waiting is permanent, and it only gets more expensive the longer you go without.
Frequently asked questions
What does Medicare Part D cover?
Part D covers outpatient prescription drugs — the medications you pick up at a pharmacy, not drugs given to you in a doctor's office or hospital. Each plan has its own formulary, or list of covered drugs, organized into cost tiers.
How much does Medicare Part D cost in 2026?
The average standalone Part D premium in 2026 is around $34.50 a month, though individual plans range from $0 to over $100. The maximum deductible any plan can charge is $615, and once you've spent $2,100 out-of-pocket on covered drugs, your plan pays 100% of covered drug costs for the rest of the year.
What happens if I don't sign up for Part D on time?
If you go 63 or more days without Part D or other creditable drug coverage after your Initial Enrollment Period ends, you may owe a late enrollment penalty added to your premium for as long as you have Part D coverage.
Is the Medicare Part D donut hole still a thing?
No. The coverage gap, or donut hole, was eliminated starting in 2025 under the Inflation Reduction Act. Part D now has a hard annual out-of-pocket cap instead — $2,100 in 2026 — after which your plan covers 100% of costs for covered drugs.
Does Medicare Part D cover weight-loss drugs like Wegovy or Zepbound?
It depends on the diagnosis, not the drug itself. Medicare Part D is generally barred from covering a drug used solely for weight loss, but the same medication may be covered when prescribed for an approved condition like type 2 diabetes or obstructive sleep apnea. Check your plan's formulary and expect a prior authorization requirement.
