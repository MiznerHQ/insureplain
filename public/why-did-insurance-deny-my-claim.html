Why Did My Insurance
Deny My Claim?

Claim Denial
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You filed a claim expecting your insurance to pay. Instead you got a letter saying denied. It feels like a betrayal — you've been paying premiums for months or years and now they won't cover you.

Here's the truth: most claim denials are not final. Insurance companies deny claims for specific, documented reasons — and most of those reasons can be challenged. Understanding exactly why your claim was denied is the first step to getting it paid.

The most common denial reasons

Why claims get denied

  • Not medically necessary — The insurer decided the treatment wasn't required based on their internal criteria. This is the number one denial reason for health claims.
  • Out-of-network provider — You saw a doctor or hospital outside your plan's network.
  • Prior authorization not obtained — Your plan required pre-approval and your doctor didn't get it.
  • Excluded service — The procedure is specifically excluded from your policy.
  • Coverage lapsed — Your policy wasn't active when the claim occurred.
  • Filing deadline missed — Most plans require claims within 90–180 days. Miss it and the claim is automatically denied.
  • Incorrect billing codes — Your provider used the wrong diagnosis or procedure code. This is a billing error, not a coverage issue, and is usually fixable.
How to fight back — step by step

The appeal process

  1. Read the denial letter in full. Identify the exact reason code and the specific policy language cited.
  2. Call your insurance company. Ask for a detailed explanation. Get the rep's name and document the call date and time.
  3. Gather your documentation. Collect doctor's notes, test results, referrals, and prior authorization approvals.
  4. Ask your doctor for a letter of medical necessity. This is a formal letter explaining why the treatment was required for your condition.
  5. File a formal internal appeal. Submit in writing with all supporting documentation. Send via certified mail.
  6. If internal appeal fails, request an external review. Under federal law you have the right to an independent review. Their decision is binding on your insurer.
  7. File a complaint with your state insurance commissioner. This often gets faster results than the appeal process alone.
What to do right now
Don't assume the denial is final. Find the reason code in your denial letter or EOB, call your insurer to confirm the reason, and ask your doctor's office if there's a billing error before filing a formal appeal. The majority of appealed claim denials are overturned — especially when your doctor provides supporting documentation. Act fast: every plan has an appeal deadline and missing it forfeits your right to contest.
Frequently asked questions
How often do insurance appeals succeed?
More often than most people expect. Studies show consumers who appeal denied health insurance claims win roughly 40–60% of the time on internal appeal alone. Most people never appeal — they assume the denial is final and pay out of pocket.
What is medical necessity and how does my insurer define it?
Medical necessity means your insurer decided a treatment isn't required based on their internal clinical guidelines — which may differ from your doctor's judgment. You can request a copy of the specific criteria they used. This is your right under federal law.
What's the difference between an internal appeal and an external review?
An internal appeal is reviewed by your insurance company. An external review is conducted by an independent organization with no financial relationship to your insurer. Under ACA rules, their decision is binding — your insurer must pay if the external reviewer overturns the denial.
