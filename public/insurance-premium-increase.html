Why Is My Insurance Premium
Going Up in 2026?

Premiums & Costs
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You opened your renewal notice expecting the same bill as last year — and instead your premium jumped. No accident, no ticket, nothing you did differently. It feels random, but it almost never is.

Insurance rates in 2026 are rising across nearly every category — health, auto, and home — for reasons that are mostly out of your control. The good news: once you know which of these reasons applies to you, there are real, specific ways to push your premium back down.

The most common reasons rates are rising

Why premiums are going up

  • Statewide rate increases — Your insurer filed for a rate hike with your state's insurance commissioner that applies to every policyholder in your category, regardless of your personal claims history.
  • Rising medical and repair costs — Hospital costs, prescription drug prices, auto parts, and labor costs have all increased — insurers pass these costs directly to premiums.
  • A claim you filed — Even a small claim can trigger a surcharge at renewal, especially for auto and home policies.
  • Someone else's claims in your area — Rising accident rates, severe weather, or theft in your zip code can raise rates for everyone nearby, not just you.
  • Loss of a discount — Discounts for bundling, good driving, or paperless billing can quietly expire or be reduced at renewal.
  • Credit-based insurance score changes — In most states, a drop in your credit score can directly increase your auto or home premium.
  • Age, life events, or coverage changes — Turning a certain age, getting married, moving, or adding a driver/dependent can shift your rate up or down.
  • Inflation adjustments on coverage limits — Insurers may automatically raise your home's insured value to match rebuilding costs, which raises the premium tied to it.
How to lower your premium — step by step

What to do about it

  1. Call your insurer and ask why. Request the specific reason code for the increase — they're required to be able to explain it.
  2. Ask about every available discount. Many discounts (safe driver, bundling, low mileage, home security systems) require you to ask — they aren't applied automatically.
  3. Raise your deductible. Increasing your deductible from $500 to $1,000 can meaningfully lower your premium if you can cover the higher out-of-pocket cost.
  4. Shop your policy every renewal. Get at least two competing quotes before you renew — loyalty rarely gets rewarded with better pricing.
  5. Review your coverage for overlap. Drop coverage you no longer need, like collision on an older car worth less than a few thousand dollars.
  6. Check your credit report. If your state allows credit-based scoring, fixing errors on your credit report can lower your rate.
  7. Ask about usage-based or telematics programs. Many auto insurers offer app-based discounts for safe driving habits.
What to do right now
Don't just pay the new amount without checking. Call your insurer and ask for the exact reason behind the increase, request a list of every discount you qualify for, and get a competing quote from at least one other insurer before your renewal date. Switching insurers is often the single biggest lever — many people save hundreds per year just by shopping around at renewal instead of auto-renewing.
Frequently asked questions
Why did my insurance premium go up if I didn't file a claim?
Statewide rate filings, rising repair/medical costs, and claims filed by other people in your area can all raise your premium even with a perfect record. Insurers price risk by category and location, not just individual history.
Will my premium keep going up every year?
Not necessarily. Rate increases tend to track inflation and claims trends industry-wide, but shopping around at each renewal and asking about discounts can offset or reverse much of the increase.
Does switching insurers actually save money?
Often, yes. Insurers price risk differently and compete for new customers with lower introductory rates. Getting a competing quote before you renew costs nothing and frequently reveals real savings.
