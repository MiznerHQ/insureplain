What Is a Health Sharing
Ministry — and Is It Real Insurance?

Health Sharing
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You've seen the ads — a "health sharing ministry" promising lower monthly costs than traditional health insurance, often tied to a faith-based or community-based model. It sounds like insurance. It gets marketed like insurance. But legally, it isn't insurance at all.

A health sharing ministry is a membership program where participants pool money and agree to help pay each other's eligible medical bills. There's no contract guaranteeing your bills get paid, and in most states, these programs are specifically exempt from insurance regulation. Understanding that distinction before you sign up — not after a claim gets denied — is the whole point of this guide.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Is a health sharing ministry legal?
Yes, it's legal in nearly every state. Most states explicitly exempt health sharing ministries from insurance regulation as long as they meet certain criteria, such as being affiliated with a religious organization.
Does the ACA penalty apply if I only have health sharing?
No federal penalty applies either way right now. The ACA's individual mandate penalty was reduced to $0 starting in 2019, so going without traditional insurance — sharing ministry or otherwise — doesn't trigger a federal tax penalty.
How much does a health sharing ministry cost per month?
Monthly "shares" typically run lower than comparable insurance premiums. Cost depends heavily on age, household size, and the specific ministry, and often increases with each renewal period.
The core difference

Why a sharing ministry isn't insurance

Traditional health insurance is a legally binding contract. If you pay your premium and the claim meets your policy terms, the insurer is legally obligated to pay. Health sharing ministries work differently — and that difference matters most when you actually need care.

  • No legal guarantee of payment — Sharing requests are reviewed and can be denied at the ministry's discretion, with no regulatory body forcing payment.
  • Not subject to ACA protections — Ministries can apply pre-existing condition exclusions, lifetime caps, or lifestyle-based restrictions that regulated insurance can't.
  • No standardized appeals process — Unlike a denied insurance claim, there's no state-mandated external review if a ministry declines to share your bill.
  • Membership, not policy — You're a "member" agreeing to share costs voluntarily, not a policyholder with enforceable coverage rights.
  • Religious or ethical eligibility rules — Many ministries require members to affirm certain lifestyle or faith commitments to join or stay enrolled.
  • State oversight varies widely — A handful of states have added consumer protection rules for ministries, but most leave them largely unregulated.
What to weigh before joining

Lower cost vs. real risk

The appeal is obvious: lower monthly costs than a marketplace plan. The tradeoff is real financial exposure if a major claim gets declined.

Often Better Than Insurance

Lower monthly cost for healthy individuals and families. Simple enrollment, often no waiting for open enrollment periods. Faith-based community appeal for some members.

Where It Falls Short

No guaranteed payment, limited or excluded coverage for chronic conditions, maternity, and mental health. No regulatory backstop if a large bill is declined.

⚠️ READ THE GUIDELINES, NOT THE MARKETING

Every legitimate ministry publishes a "guidelines" or "member handbook" document listing exactly what is and isn't eligible for sharing. Read this in full before enrolling — it's the closest thing to a policy document they have, and it's where the real exclusions live.

Before you enroll

How to evaluate a health sharing ministry

  1. Find the published guidelines document. If a ministry won't show you detailed eligibility rules before you pay anything, treat that as a red flag.
  2. Check their financial track record. Look for how long they've operated and whether they publish how much they actually share each year versus what they collect.
  3. Confirm pre-existing condition rules. Most ministries impose waiting periods or permanent exclusions for conditions you had before joining — get the exact terms in writing.
  4. Ask about the appeals process. Find out exactly what happens if a sharing request is declined and whether there's any internal review step.
  5. Compare against a real marketplace quote. Check what a subsidized marketplace plan would actually cost you before assuming the ministry is cheaper.
  6. Understand your tax situation. Confirm whether your state currently allows a deduction for ministry payments — this varies and changes over time.
What to do right now
Before enrolling, request the ministry's current member guidelines in writing and read the exclusions section first — not the homepage. If you have any chronic condition, are planning a pregnancy, or want guaranteed coverage, get a real marketplace insurance quote to compare against. A health sharing ministry can work well for healthy individuals comfortable with some risk, but it is not a substitute for insurance if you need certainty that a major bill will be paid.
Frequently asked questions
Is a health sharing ministry the same as health insurance?
No. Health sharing ministries are not insurance and are not regulated as insurance in most states. Members voluntarily share medical costs with each other, but there is no legal guarantee that your bills will be paid.
Are health sharing ministry payments tax deductible?
It depends on your state and the IRS rules at the time. Some states have passed tax parity laws allowing deductions similar to insurance premiums, but this is not universal, so check current state and federal guidance before assuming a deduction applies.
What does a health sharing ministry typically not cover?
Most ministries exclude or limit coverage for pre-existing conditions, mental health care, maternity (unless conceived after joining), preventive care, and routine prescriptions. Always read the published guidelines, not just the marketing material.
Can I be denied if I get sick after joining a health sharing ministry?
Yes. Because ministries are not bound by ACA protections, they can deny sharing requests based on medical history, lifestyle, or their own internal guidelines. There is no formal appeals process required by law like there is with regulated insurance.
