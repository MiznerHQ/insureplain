What Is Prior Authorization and Why Was It Denied?

Your doctor ordered it. Your insurance said no. Here's what that means and what you can do.

You need a procedure, medication, or specialist visit. Your doctor puts in the order. Then you get a letter — or your doctor's office calls — saying your insurance denied prior authorization. What does that even mean?

Prior authorization (also called prior auth, pre-auth, or pre-certification) is your insurance company's way of saying: before we agree to pay for this, we need to approve it first. It's one of the most frustrating parts of the American health insurance system — and denials happen more than most people realize.

What Is Prior Authorization?

Prior authorization is a requirement that your doctor get approval from your insurance company before you receive certain types of care. It applies to things like surgeries, brand-name medications, MRIs and other imaging, specialist referrals, and some mental health services.

💡 Prior auth isn't your doctor's decision — it's a gate your insurer puts up to control costs. Your doctor may fully recommend the treatment, but your insurance still has to sign off before they'll pay.

Why Do Insurance Companies Require It?

Insurers say prior authorization ensures treatments are medically necessary and that cheaper alternatives have been tried first. Critics argue it's primarily a cost-control tool that delays and denies legitimate care.

Either way, it's standard practice across most health plans — and if your plan requires it and you skip it, you could end up paying the full cost yourself.

What Should You Do If It's Denied?

Step 1: Get the denial in writing. Your insurer must tell you why they denied it. Ask for the denial letter if you haven't received one.

Step 2: Call your doctor's office. They deal with prior auth denials constantly. They may be able to resubmit with additional clinical notes or a peer-to-peer review — where your doctor speaks directly with the insurance company's medical reviewer.

Step 3: File an internal appeal. You have the legal right to appeal any denial. Your denial letter will explain how. Your insurer must respond within a set timeframe.

Step 4: Request an external review. If your internal appeal is denied, you can request an independent external review. A third party — not your insurer — makes the final call. Insurers lose these more often than you'd think.

⚠️ Don't wait passively. Prior auth denials have strict appeal deadlines — sometimes as short as 30 days. If you need urgent care, ask about an expedited appeal, which insurers must process within 72 hours.

What Is a Peer-to-Peer Review?

A peer-to-peer review is when your doctor calls the insurance company's medical director to argue your case directly. It's one of the most effective tools for overturning a denial. Ask your doctor's office if they've requested one — many offices will do this automatically, but some need to be asked.

What If You Can't Wait for the Appeal?

If your condition is urgent, ask your doctor about an expedited appeal. Federal law requires insurers to respond to expedited appeals within 72 hours. If your health is at serious risk, you may also be able to get emergency authorization.

Can You Avoid Prior Auth Denials in the Future?

Not always — but these steps help: confirm with your doctor's office that prior auth has been submitted before any procedure, ask your insurer directly whether a service requires prior auth, and stay in-network whenever possible. Out-of-network care triggers additional scrutiny.
