Prior Authorization Denied?
Here's Exactly What to Do

Prior Authorization
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

Your doctor ordered a treatment, procedure, or medication. Before you could get it, your insurance required advance approval — called prior authorization. And they said no.

A prior auth denial doesn't mean the treatment is wrong or that your doctor made a mistake. It means your insurer reviewed the request against their internal criteria and decided it didn't meet their definition of medically necessary — at least not yet. Most prior auth denials can be successfully appealed, especially when your doctor provides supporting documentation.

Why prior auth gets denied

The most common reasons

  • Not medically necessary — The insurer's clinical guidelines don't support the treatment for your diagnosis. This is the most common reason and the most commonly overturned on appeal.
  • Step therapy required — Your plan requires you to try a cheaper treatment first before approving the one your doctor requested. Also called "fail first."
  • Missing documentation — The request was incomplete. Your doctor's office may not have submitted all required clinical notes or test results.
  • Wrong diagnosis code — A billing error on the request — the wrong ICD-10 code — can trigger an automatic denial even when the treatment is appropriate.
  • Not a covered benefit — The specific treatment is excluded from your plan entirely, regardless of medical need.
  • Out-of-network provider — The provider who would perform the procedure isn't in your network and your plan requires in-network care.
  • Experimental or investigational — Your insurer classified the treatment as not yet proven, even if your doctor considers it standard of care.
Time is critical

Deadlines you cannot miss

Standard appeal

Most plans require you to file an internal appeal within 180 days of the denial. Missing this deadline forfeits your right to appeal.

Urgent care appeal

If your situation is urgent, you can request an expedited review. Insurers must respond within 72 hours under federal law.

⚠️ Don't wait

Call your insurer the same day you receive the denial. Ask for the specific reason code and the clinical criteria they used. You need this information to build your appeal.

How to fight the denial — step by step

The appeal process

  1. Get the denial in writing. The denial letter must state the specific reason and the clinical criteria used. If you only got a phone call, request the written denial immediately.
  2. Call your insurer and ask for the exact criteria. Ask them to read you the specific guideline your request failed to meet. Document the rep's name, date, and time.
  3. Call your doctor's office the same day. Tell them the denial reason. Ask them to submit a letter of medical necessity and any missing clinical documentation.
  4. Check if step therapy applies. If your plan requires you to try another treatment first, ask your doctor if there's a medical reason you can't — that's grounds for a step therapy exception.
  5. File a formal internal appeal in writing. Include your doctor's letter, clinical notes, test results, and any medical literature supporting the treatment. Send via certified mail.
  6. If internal appeal fails, request an external review. An independent organization reviews the case. Their decision is binding on your insurer under federal law.
  7. File a complaint with your state insurance commissioner. Do this in parallel — it often accelerates resolution and creates a formal record.
What to do right now
Don't accept the denial as final. Call your insurer today, get the specific denial reason in writing, and immediately loop in your doctor's office. The most powerful thing in a prior auth appeal is a detailed letter of medical necessity from your physician — it directly addresses the insurer's criteria and is the single biggest factor in getting denials overturned. Act within days, not weeks.
Frequently asked questions
Can I get treatment while my appeal is pending?
Sometimes. If your condition is urgent, you can request an expedited appeal and ask your doctor to document why you can't wait. In some cases your doctor can also request a peer-to-peer review — a direct call between your physician and the insurer's medical reviewer — which often resolves denials faster than a formal appeal.
What is a peer-to-peer review?
A peer-to-peer review is a direct conversation between your doctor and the insurance company's medical director. Your doctor makes the clinical case directly. This is one of the most effective tools for overturning prior auth denials and can often happen within 24–48 hours of a denial. Ask your doctor's office to request one immediately.
What if my plan says the treatment is experimental?
Ask your doctor to provide medical literature showing the treatment is considered standard of care by major medical associations. Insurers are required to consider peer-reviewed clinical evidence in their review. An external review is particularly effective for experimental treatment denials because the independent reviewer applies clinical standards, not your insurer's internal guidelines.
