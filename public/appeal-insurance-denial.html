How to Appeal an
Insurance Denial

Claim Denial
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You got a denial letter and it feels like the end of the road. It isn't. Insurance companies are required by law to give you the right to appeal every denial — and the process is more straightforward than they want you to think.

The key is acting fast, documenting everything, and knowing exactly what to say. Most people never appeal because they assume the denial is final. That assumption costs them thousands of dollars.

Before you appeal — gather these

What you need first

  • Your denial letter — Find the exact reason code and policy language cited
  • Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) — This shows what was billed, what was paid, and why it was denied
  • Your insurance policy — The actual document, not just your ID card
  • Doctor's notes and records — Any clinical documentation supporting the treatment
  • A letter of medical necessity — Ask your doctor to write this specifically for your appeal
  • Prior authorization approval — If you got one, include it. If you didn't, explain why
The appeal process — step by step

How to file your appeal

  1. Find your appeal deadline. Most plans give you 180 days from the denial date. Missing this deadline forfeits your right to appeal entirely. Check your denial letter for the exact date.
  2. Call your insurer first. Ask for a detailed explanation of the denial. Get the rep's full name, employee ID, and the date and time of the call. Write it all down.
  3. Request the specific criteria used. Under federal law you can request the exact clinical guidelines your insurer used to deny the claim. This is your right — ask for it in writing.
  4. Get a letter of medical necessity from your doctor. This is a formal letter stating why the treatment was medically required for your specific condition. It is the single most powerful document in an appeal.
  5. Write your appeal letter. State your name, policy number, claim number, and denial reason. Then explain clearly why the denial was incorrect, citing your policy language and your doctor's documentation.
  6. Submit everything in writing via certified mail. Keep copies of every document. Certified mail creates a paper trail your insurer cannot deny receiving.
  7. Follow up in writing every 2 weeks. Insurers are required to respond within specific timeframes — 30 days for non-urgent claims, 72 hours for urgent ones. If they miss the deadline, document it.
  8. If internal appeal fails, request an external review. An independent organization reviews your case. Their decision is binding on your insurer under federal law.
What to do right now
Find your denial letter right now and locate the appeal deadline — that date is everything. Call your insurer today to request the clinical criteria used to deny your claim. Then call your doctor's office and ask them to prepare a letter of medical necessity. Do not wait. Every day you delay is a day closer to losing your right to appeal.
Frequently asked questions
How long does an insurance appeal take?
Internal appeals for non-urgent claims must be decided within 30 days. Urgent or emergency claims must be decided within 72 hours. If your insurer misses these deadlines, document it and escalate to your state insurance commissioner immediately.
What is an external review and when can I request one?
An external review is conducted by an independent organization with no financial relationship to your insurer. You can typically request one after your internal appeal is denied, or if your insurer fails to respond within the required timeframe. Their decision is legally binding on your insurance company.
Should I hire a patient advocate or attorney?
For large claims — anything over $5,000 — a patient advocate or healthcare attorney can significantly improve your odds. Many work on contingency, meaning they only get paid if you win. For smaller claims, a strong letter of medical necessity from your doctor is usually enough.
