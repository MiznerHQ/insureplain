Copay vs Coinsurance:
What's the Difference?

Costs & Billing
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.

The Short Answer

The Quick Difference

A copay is a flat dollar amount you pay for a specific service — like $30 every time you see your primary doctor, no matter what the visit actually costs.

A coinsurance is a percentage of the cost you're responsible for after you've met your deductible — like 20% of a $5,000 hospital bill. The bigger the bill, the bigger your share.

Side By Side

Copay vs Coinsurance

Copay

Fixed dollar amount. Same every time, regardless of the actual cost of the service. Usually applies to routine visits, urgent care, and prescriptions.

Coinsurance

A percentage of the bill. Varies based on what the service actually costs. Usually applies to bigger-ticket items like surgery, hospital stays, and specialist procedures.

How They Work Together

Do I Pay Both?

Often, yes — just not for the same service at the same time. A typical plan might charge you a copay for office visits and prescriptions, but switch to coinsurance once you're dealing with something bigger like surgery, an MRI, or a hospital admission.

TIMING MATTERS

Coinsurance almost always kicks in only after you've met your annual deductible. Copays are different — many plans charge them right away, deductible met or not. Check your plan's Summary of Benefits to see how yours is structured.

Quick Example

A Real Example

Say you need outpatient surgery that costs $5,000. Your plan has a $1,500 deductible and 20% coinsurance.

  1. You pay the first $1,500 yourself — that's your deductible, separate from coinsurance.
  2. That leaves $3,500 of the bill still to settle.
  3. Your coinsurance is 20% of that remaining amount: $700.
  4. Your insurer covers the other 80%: $2,800.
  5. Your total out-of-pocket cost for this procedure: $2,200 ($1,500 deductible + $700 coinsurance).

Common Questions

FAQ

Do I pay a copay and coinsurance for the same visit?

Usually not for a single service — most plans apply one or the other per service type. But over the course of a year, you could pay copays for routine visits and coinsurance for a bigger procedure like surgery.

Does my copay count toward my deductible?

Often no. Many plans apply copays even before you've met your deductible, since copays aren't technically part of the deductible calculation. Coinsurance, by contrast, almost always applies after the deductible is met.

Which is cheaper — copay or coinsurance?

It depends on the cost of the service. For a routine $30 office visit, a flat copay and a small coinsurance percentage might cost about the same. But for a $50,000 hospital stay, a capped copay protects you far better than 20% coinsurance, which could mean $10,000 out of pocket.

Does coinsurance stop once I hit my out-of-pocket maximum?

Yes. Once you reach your plan's out-of-pocket maximum for the year, your insurer covers 100% of covered costs for the rest of the plan year — no more copays or coinsurance.
