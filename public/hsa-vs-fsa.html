What Is an
HSA vs FSA?

Health Accounts

What's actually happening

You're staring at open enrollment paperwork, or maybe a new-hire benefits packet, and there it is: a choice between an HSA and an FSA. Both promise to save you money on taxes. Both let you pay for medical costs with money that never touches your paycheck's tax bill. And the acronyms are close enough that it's easy to assume they're basically the same thing with a different name.

They're not. An HSA (Health Savings Account) and an FSA (Flexible Spending Account) both use pre-tax dollars to cover medical expenses, but they differ in who owns the account, whether your unused balance disappears at year's end, whether you need a specific type of health plan to qualify, and how much you're allowed to set aside each year. Picking the wrong one — or picking one when you didn't need to give up the other — can cost you real money, either through forfeited funds or a missed opportunity to build long-term, tax-free savings.

This guide breaks down exactly how each account works, what changed for the 2026 tax year, and how to figure out which one actually fits your situation.

Quick answers

What people ask most

CAN YOU HAVE AN HSA AND FSA AT THE SAME TIME?
Generally, no — not with a standard FSA. If you're contributing to an HSA, you can typically only pair it with a limited-purpose FSA that covers dental and vision expenses. A general-purpose FSA alongside an HSA can disqualify your HSA contributions entirely, so check your specific plan rules first.
HSA VS FSA: WHICH IS BETTER?
It depends on your health plan and spending pattern, not a universal winner. An HSA tends to favor people on a high-deductible health plan who want their savings to roll over and grow long-term. An FSA can suit anyone with predictable annual medical costs who wants their full election available on day one.
WHAT ARE THE HSA AND FSA CONTRIBUTION LIMITS FOR 2026?
For 2026, HSA limits are $4,400 for self-only coverage and $8,750 for family coverage, plus a $1,000 catch-up if you're 55 or older. The FSA limit for 2026 is $3,400 per employee, regardless of whether you're covering just yourself or a family.
The core differences

HSA vs FSA: how they actually work

Both accounts let you set aside pre-tax money for qualified medical expenses, and the list of eligible expenses is nearly identical — doctor visits, prescriptions, dental and vision care, and many over-the-counter items count toward either account. The real differences show up in ownership, timing, and what happens to the money you don't spend.

  • Ownership — An HSA belongs to you personally, the same way a bank account does. An FSA is owned and administered by your employer, and it doesn't travel with you if you leave your job.
  • Eligibility — HSAs require you to be enrolled in a qualifying high-deductible health plan (HDHP). FSAs don't have this requirement and are generally available with most employer-sponsored health plans, including PPOs and HMOs.
  • Rollover — HSA balances roll over every year with no limit or expiration. FSA funds are typically subject to a use-it-or-lose-it rule, though some employers allow a limited carryover or a short grace period.
  • Fund availability — With an FSA, your full annual election is usually available on day one of the plan year. With an HSA, you can only spend what you've actually contributed so far.
  • Investment potential — HSA funds can typically be invested once your balance reaches a certain threshold, letting the account function similarly to a retirement vehicle. FSAs don't offer this option.
  • Portability — If you change jobs or retire, your HSA moves with you. An FSA generally stays with your former employer, and any remaining balance is usually forfeited.
2026 numbers

Contribution limits and what changed this year

Every year the IRS adjusts these limits for inflation, and 2026 brought a meaningful bump for both account types. Here's where things stand:

HSA — 2026

$4,400 for self-only coverage, $8,750 for family coverage. Those 55 and older can contribute an additional $1,000 catch-up. Employer contributions count toward these limits.

FSA — 2026

$3,400 per employee, regardless of coverage tier. If your employer allows carryover, up to $680 can move into the next plan year.

⚠️ HDHP REQUIREMENT FOR HSA ELIGIBILITY

To contribute to an HSA in 2026, your health plan must have a minimum deductible of $1,700 for self-only coverage or $3,400 for family coverage, along with an out-of-pocket maximum no higher than $8,500 (self-only) or $17,000 (family). Confirm your specific plan meets these thresholds before assuming you're eligible.

How to decide

Choosing between HSA and FSA — step by step

  1. Check your health plan type first. If you're not enrolled in a qualifying HDHP, an HSA isn't available to you this year regardless of preference — the FSA becomes your default option if your employer offers one.
  2. Estimate your predictable annual medical costs. If you know you'll have recurring expenses like prescriptions, contacts, or routine dental work, an FSA's day-one fund availability can be genuinely useful.
  3. Think about how long you want the money to sit. If you're generally healthy and want a long-term, tax-advantaged savings vehicle, the HSA's indefinite rollover and investment options carry real long-term value.
  4. Consider job stability and portability. If you expect to change jobs, an HSA's portability means the account and its balance follow you. An FSA does not.
  5. Ask about a limited-purpose FSA. If you have an HSA-eligible HDHP but also want to set aside pre-tax money specifically for dental or vision costs, ask your employer whether a limited-purpose FSA is available alongside your HSA.
  6. Review your plan's specific carryover and grace period rules. Not all employers offer the same FSA flexibility, so confirm what happens to unused funds before you commit to a contribution amount.
What to do right now
Confirm whether your current health plan qualifies as an HDHP before assuming you're eligible for an HSA. If you're weighing your deductible against your expected medical spending, run the numbers for both scenarios before your enrollment deadline — once the plan year starts, you generally can't switch your election outside of a qualifying life event. If you're unsure which account fits, your HR department or benefits administrator can usually walk through your specific plan's rules.
Frequently asked questions
Can I have both an HSA and an FSA at the same time?
Generally no, not with a standard health FSA. If you have an HSA, you can typically only pair it with a limited-purpose FSA that covers dental and vision expenses only. Combining an HSA with a general-purpose FSA can make you ineligible to contribute to your HSA, so check your plan documents or ask your HR department before enrolling in both.
What happens to unused HSA or FSA money at the end of the year?
HSA funds never expire and roll over indefinitely, year after year, even if you change jobs or health plans. FSA funds are generally subject to a use-it-or-lose-it rule, though some employers offer a limited carryover amount or a short grace period. Any FSA balance beyond that is typically forfeited.
Is an HSA or FSA better for me?
It depends on your health plan and how predictable your medical expenses are. An HSA tends to work well for people enrolled in a high-deductible health plan who want a long-term, portable savings option. An FSA can work well for anyone with predictable annual expenses who wants access to the full election amount early in the plan year. Reviewing your own plan options and expected costs is the best way to decide.
Do I need a high-deductible health plan to get an HSA?
Yes. To contribute to an HSA, you must be enrolled in a qualifying high-deductible health plan (HDHP) and cannot have other disqualifying coverage. An FSA does not have this requirement and is typically available with most employer-sponsored health plans.
What can I actually buy with HSA or FSA funds?
Both accounts generally cover the same categories of IRS-qualified medical expenses, including doctor visits, prescriptions, dental and vision care, and many over-the-counter health products. The eligible expense list is largely identical between the two account types; the real differences are in ownership, rollover, and eligibility rules, not what you're allowed to buy.
