What Is a Life Insurance
Beneficiary?

Life Insurance

What's actually happening

You bought a life insurance policy — maybe years ago — and named a beneficiary at the time. Life has changed since then: a marriage, a divorce, a new child, a falling out. Now you're wondering if the person listed on your policy is still who you'd actually want receiving the payout.

A beneficiary is simply the person or entity your insurer pays when you die. Who you named, how you named them, and whether you've kept it updated all directly determine where that money goes — sometimes overriding what your will says entirely.

Quick answers

What people ask most

WHAT IS A BENEFICIARY IN LIFE INSURANCE?
A beneficiary is the person or entity named to receive your policy's death benefit. You choose them when you apply for coverage, and you can typically update the designation at any time while the policy is active.
WHAT'S THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN A PRIMARY AND CONTINGENT BENEFICIARY?
A primary beneficiary is first in line to receive the payout; a contingent beneficiary only receives it if the primary is unavailable. "Unavailable" usually means they passed away before you or cannot be located.
HOW DO I CHANGE MY LIFE INSURANCE BENEFICIARY?
Most insurers let you change your beneficiary by submitting a beneficiary change form, either through your online account, your agent, or by mailing a signed form directly to the insurer.
Types of beneficiaries

Primary vs. contingent — and revocable vs. irrevocable

Every policy structures its beneficiaries in tiers, and the terms attached to each name determine how much control you retain over the designation going forward.

Primary beneficiary

First in line for the death benefit. You can name one person or split the payout by percentage across multiple primary beneficiaries.

Contingent beneficiary

Receives the payout only if all primary beneficiaries are unavailable. Acts as a backup layer so the money doesn't default to your estate.

⚠️ REVOCABLE VS. IRREVOCABLE

Most beneficiaries are revocable, meaning you can change them anytime without their consent. An irrevocable beneficiary must approve any changes to the designation or the policy itself — this is less common and usually tied to a legal agreement, like a divorce settlement.

Why this matters more than people think

Beneficiary designations can override your will

Life insurance proceeds generally bypass probate and go directly to whoever is named on the policy — regardless of what your will says. This trips people up after a divorce or remarriage: an ex-spouse named years ago can still legally receive the payout if the designation was never updated.

  • No named beneficiary — the payout typically defaults to your estate and goes through probate, which can delay distribution.
  • Outdated designation — an ex-spouse, estranged relative, or even a deceased person can still be legally entitled to the payout if never updated.
  • Minor children as beneficiaries — insurers generally won't pay a death benefit directly to a minor; a guardian or trust is usually required.
  • Naming a trust — some people name a trust as beneficiary for more control over how and when funds are distributed, especially with minor children involved.
  • Estate planning overlap — beneficiary designations work alongside broader documents like a policy rider or your will, but typically take precedence for the payout itself.
Changing your beneficiary

Step by step

  1. Log into your policy's online portal if your insurer offers one — many allow beneficiary changes without any paperwork.
  2. Request a beneficiary change form from your agent or insurer if online access isn't available.
  3. Decide primary vs. contingent and how you want the payout split if naming more than one person.
  4. Fill out the form completely — full legal names, dates of birth, and relationship to you are typically required.
  5. Submit and confirm — request written confirmation from the insurer that the change was processed and is on file.
  6. Review after major life events — marriage, divorce, a new child, or a death in the family are all good triggers to double-check your designation.
What to do right now
Pull up your current policy documents and confirm exactly who is listed as your primary and contingent beneficiary today. If it's been more than a couple of years, or if your life circumstances have changed since you last checked, contact your insurer directly to update the designation — it's usually a quick form, not a full underwriting process.
Frequently asked questions
What happens if I don't name a beneficiary on my life insurance policy?
If no beneficiary is named, or all named beneficiaries have passed away, the death benefit typically goes to your estate instead. That usually means the payout goes through probate, which can delay distribution and expose the funds to creditors or estate taxes depending on your state.
Can I name more than one beneficiary on a life insurance policy?
Yes, most insurers let you name multiple primary beneficiaries and assign each a specific percentage of the death benefit. You can also name multiple contingent beneficiaries in case a primary beneficiary is unavailable to receive the payout.
Does my beneficiary need to know they're listed on my policy?
No, insurers generally don't require your beneficiary's knowledge or consent for you to name them. However, it's worth telling them so they know to file a claim and can locate the policy details when the time comes.
Can a beneficiary designation override my will?
In most cases, yes — the beneficiary named directly on the policy typically takes priority over instructions in a will. This is why keeping your beneficiary designations updated after major life events like divorce or remarriage matters as much as updating your will.
