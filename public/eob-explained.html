What Is an EOB?
Your Explanation of Benefits, Decoded

Health Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You go to the doctor, get treated, and a few weeks later a confusing document shows up in your mailbox or patient portal. It lists charges, adjustments, and amounts — and none of the numbers match what you thought you'd owe.

That document is your Explanation of Benefits, or EOB. It is not a bill. It's a summary from your insurance company showing what your provider charged, what your insurer paid, what was adjusted off, and what — if anything — you still owe. Understanding it is the key to catching billing errors before you pay them.

The key sections of every EOB

What each line means

  • Amount Billed — The full price your provider charged before any discounts or insurance adjustments. This number is almost always inflated and is rarely what anyone actually pays.
  • Allowed Amount — The negotiated rate your insurer has agreed to pay for that service. In-network providers have agreed to accept this lower amount as payment in full.
  • Plan Paid — What your insurance company actually paid your provider after applying your deductible, copay, and coinsurance rules.
  • Adjustment / Discount — The difference between the billed amount and the allowed amount. This gets written off — you never owe it.
  • Your Responsibility — The amount you actually owe. This is what matters. It reflects your deductible, copay, or coinsurance portion of the allowed amount.
  • Deductible Applied — How much of this claim was applied toward your annual deductible. Once your deductible is met, your share of future claims drops significantly.
  • Remark / Reason Codes — Short codes explaining why the claim was processed the way it was. If you see a denial or reduction here, these codes tell you why.
Common confusion points

Why the numbers look wrong

Not a bill

The EOB arrives before your provider's bill. Do not pay anything until you receive an actual invoice from the provider — and compare it to your EOB first.

Numbers don't match

If your provider's bill is higher than the "Your Responsibility" amount on your EOB, call your provider. They may have billed you for an amount your insurer already adjusted off.

⚠️ Watch for this

If a service shows as denied on your EOB but your doctor said it was covered, don't pay the bill yet. Call your insurer first — it may be a billing code error that can be resubmitted and paid.

How to read it — step by step

What to do when your EOB arrives

  1. Confirm the service is yours. Check the date, provider name, and procedure. Medical identity theft shows up on EOBs first.
  2. Find the "Your Responsibility" line. That's the only number you may owe — not the billed amount.
  3. Check the deductible applied line. Track this against your annual deductible so you know how close you are to full coverage kicking in.
  4. Look for denial or remark codes. If anything was denied or reduced, find the code and look it up — or paste it into the InsurePlain decoder below.
  5. Wait for the provider bill before paying anything. Compare the bill to your EOB. They should match on the "Your Responsibility" line.
  6. If numbers don't match, call your provider first. Most discrepancies are billing errors, not coverage disputes.
What to do right now
Pull out your most recent EOB and find the "Your Responsibility" line. That's the only amount you owe — ignore the billed amount entirely. If you see any denial codes, look them up before paying anything. If your provider's bill doesn't match your EOB, call the provider's billing department and read them the allowed amount from your EOB. Most overcharges get corrected immediately.
Frequently asked questions
Is an EOB the same as a bill?
No. An EOB is a summary from your insurance company — it shows what was processed but it is not a request for payment. Your actual bill comes separately from your doctor or hospital. Always wait for the provider bill before paying anything.
What if my EOB shows a service as denied?
A denial on your EOB doesn't mean you automatically owe the full amount. Call your insurer and ask for the specific reason. Many denials are caused by billing code errors that can be corrected and resubmitted — and then paid.
How long do I have to dispute something on my EOB?
Most plans give you 180 days from the date of service to file an appeal. Don't wait — if something looks wrong on your EOB, call your insurer as soon as possible to start the dispute process.
