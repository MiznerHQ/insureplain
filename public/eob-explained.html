What Does EOB Mean on My Insurance Statement?

It's not a bill — but it's telling you something important. Here's how to read it.

You open the mail and find a document from your insurance company. At the top it says Explanation of Benefits — or just EOB. It's full of confusing columns, codes, and dollar amounts. And somewhere it might say "This is not a bill."

So what is it? And should you care?

Yes — you should care. An EOB is one of the most important documents your insurer sends you, and a lot of people throw it away without reading it. That's a mistake.

What Is an EOB?

An EOB is a summary that your insurance company sends you after you receive medical care. It explains how a claim was processed — what your provider billed, what your insurance agreed to pay, and what (if anything) you owe.

💡 Think of it like a receipt from your insurance company. It's showing you the math behind your claim — not asking you for money (yet).

You usually receive an EOB within a few weeks of a doctor's visit, hospital stay, lab test, or prescription. Most insurers also let you view them online through your member portal.

What Are the Key Columns on an EOB?

Column What It Means
Billed Amount What your provider originally charged — often an inflated "sticker price"
Allowed Amount The negotiated rate your insurer agreed to pay — almost always lower than billed
Plan Paid What your insurance company actually paid the provider
Your Responsibility What you owe — this is what you'll see on the actual bill from your provider
Deductible Applied How much of this claim counted toward your annual deductible
Copay / Coinsurance Your fixed or percentage share of the allowed amount
Not Covered Any charges your plan refused to pay — check the reason code

Why Does the Billed Amount Look So High?

Hospitals and providers often charge far more than they expect to receive. It's a negotiating strategy — the "sticker price" is rarely what anyone actually pays. Your insurer has a contract with in-network providers that sets the allowed amount, which is the real number that matters.

If your provider billed $900 but the allowed amount is $310, your insurer essentially wiped out $590. You only owe your share of the $310.

Why Does the EOB Say "This Is Not a Bill"?

Because it isn't one. The EOB comes from your insurer. The actual bill comes from your doctor or hospital separately. They'll send you a statement showing what you owe after insurance has been applied.

📋 The EOB tells you what's coming. The provider bill is the actual request for payment. Match them up — the "Your Responsibility" column on your EOB should match what your doctor bills you.

What Are Reason Codes?

If a claim was denied or only partially paid, your EOB will include a reason code — a short code that explains why. Common ones include:

Code Type What It Usually Means
Not medically necessary Insurer decided the service wasn't required for your condition
Out-of-network Provider wasn't in your plan's network — you may owe more
Prior authorization required Your doctor needed approval before the service
Duplicate claim Same service was billed twice
Benefit maximum reached You've hit your plan's coverage limit for this type of service

If you see a reason you don't understand, call your insurer. The number is on the back of your insurance card.

When Should You Be Concerned About an EOB?

⚠️ Red flags to watch for: services you don't recognize, a billed amount that looks wrong, a denial that surprises you, or a "Your Responsibility" amount that's way higher than you expected. These are worth investigating — billing errors are more common than people think.

Steps to take if something looks off:

1. Compare your EOB to your actual provider bill. They should match. If the numbers differ, call the provider's billing department first.

2. Check the date and service description. Make sure the service listed matches what you actually received and when.

3. Look up the reason code. Your insurer's website usually has a full glossary. Or just call and ask them to explain it in plain English.

4. If you were denied and disagree, appeal. You have the right to appeal any denied claim. The EOB will usually explain how.

Does Every Insurance Type Send an EOB?

Health insurance always sends EOBs. Dental and vision insurance usually do too. Auto and homeowners insurance work differently — they send claims summaries, but they're typically called something else.

Some Medicare Advantage and Medicaid plans send EOBs. Original Medicare sends a similar document called a Medicare Summary Notice (MSN).

Should You Save Your EOBs?

Yes — at least until you've paid the corresponding provider bill and confirmed the amounts match. If you have ongoing treatment or a complex claim, keep EOBs for the full year in case you need them for taxes or a dispute.
