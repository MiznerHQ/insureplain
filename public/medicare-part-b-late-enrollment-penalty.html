Medicare Part B Late
Enrollment Penalty

MEDICARE

Written by the InsurePlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

If you missed your window to sign up for Medicare Part B and didn't have qualifying employer coverage to fall back on, Medicare adds a permanent penalty to your monthly premium. It isn't a one-time fine — it's baked into your premium for as long as you have Part B, in most cases for life.

The good news: the math behind the penalty is simple and predictable once you understand how it's calculated. The bad news: a lot of people don't find out they owe it until their first premium bill arrives, which is why understanding the rules ahead of time matters so much.

This guide breaks down exactly how the penalty is calculated, walks through real examples, covers the narrow exceptions that can protect you, and explains what a rare successful appeal actually looks like.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Is there a Part B penalty calculator?
Yes, and you can also do the math yourself. Count the full 12-month periods you delayed, multiply that number by 10%, then apply that percentage to the current standard Part B premium ($202.90 in 2026).
How is the penalty actually calculated?
10% of the standard premium per full year of delay. Medicare only counts complete 12-month periods — partial years don't add to the penalty, so timing your enrollment even a few months earlier can matter.
Can I appeal the penalty?
In limited cases, yes. If a federal employee gave you incorrect information that caused you to miss your window, you can request equitable relief. Most appeals require documentation and aren't guaranteed to succeed.
How it's calculated

The math behind your penalty

Medicare's formula is straightforward once you see it laid out. For every full 12-month period you were eligible for Part B but didn't enroll — and didn't have creditable coverage — your premium goes up by 10%, permanently. Whether this applies to you often comes down to whether your employer insurance counted as active, creditable coverage during that gap.

  • Standard 2026 premium: $202.90/month — the baseline every penalty percentage is applied to.
  • 10% per full year — Medicare rounds down, so 23 months of delay counts as one full year, not two.
  • 1 year late — adds roughly $20.29/month, bringing your premium to about $223.19.
  • 2 years late — adds roughly $40.58/month, for a premium around $243.48.
  • 5 years late — adds roughly $101.45/month, more than half your base premium again.
  • The percentage is permanent — but the dollar amount recalculates each year as the standard premium changes.
On time vs. late

What timing actually costs you

The difference between enrolling during your Initial Enrollment Period and waiting even one extra year is more than just inconvenience — it compounds every single month you have Medicare.

Enroll on time

You pay the standard $202.90/month premium in 2026, with no surcharge added, ever, for Part B.

2 years late

You pay $243.48/month for life — an extra $487.60 every year, indefinitely, for missing your window.

⚠️ EXCEPTIONS THAT PROTECT YOU

You can delay Part B penalty-free only if you have active, current employer group health coverage — through your own job or a spouse's — at a company with 20 or more employees. Retiree coverage and COBRA do not qualify, which is one of the most common mistakes people make. If you're weighing switching from Medicare Advantage to Medigap, note that your Part B enrollment status and any penalty carry over regardless of which path you take.

If you've already been penalized

Steps to take right now

  1. STEP 1. Confirm the penalty on your Medicare premium bill or your Social Security benefit statement, since the exact percentage is disclosed there.
  2. STEP 2. Gather documentation of any employer coverage you had during the gap — this is the strongest evidence if you believe the penalty was applied incorrectly.
  3. STEP 3. If you believe you received incorrect guidance from Social Security or Medicare, request an equitable relief review in writing at your local Social Security office.
  4. STEP 4. If no exception or appeal applies, plan for the penalty as a permanent line item — it won't disappear on its own, and it recalculates (not disappears) each year.
  5. STEP 5. Compare your options during open enrollment to make sure the rest of your coverage still fits your needs — and if you've already missed open enrollment entirely, know what alternate windows are still available to you.
  6. STEP 6. If you're helping a family member navigate Medigap alongside this, our Plan G vs. Plan N comparison covers how supplemental coverage interacts with Part B costs.
What to do right now
If you're approaching your 65th birthday, mark your 7-month Initial Enrollment Period on a calendar now — 3 months before, your birthday month, and 3 months after. If you've already missed it, calculate your likely penalty using the formula above so there are no surprises on your first bill, and check whether you qualify for any of the narrow exceptions before assuming the penalty is unavoidable.
Frequently asked questions
How is the Medicare Part B late enrollment penalty calculated?
Medicare adds 10% of the standard Part B premium for every full 12-month period you were eligible but didn't enroll and didn't have creditable coverage. In 2026, the standard premium is $202.90, so each 12-month delay adds about $20.29 to your monthly premium, permanently.
Is there a Medicare Part B penalty calculator I can use?
Yes — Medicare.gov and several licensed broker sites offer penalty calculators where you enter how many months you delayed enrollment. The math is straightforward enough to do yourself: count full 12-month periods of delay, multiply by 10%, then multiply that percentage by the current standard Part B premium.
Can I appeal or get the Part B penalty waived?
Appeals are possible but uncommon. You can request an equitable relief review if you believe you received incorrect information from a federal employee (like Social Security) that caused you to miss your enrollment window. You'll need to submit a written request with supporting documentation to your local Social Security office.
What are the exceptions to the Part B late enrollment penalty?
The main exception is having active, current employer group health coverage (through your own or a spouse's job at a company with 20+ employees) at the time you delayed enrolling. Retiree coverage and COBRA do not count as active coverage and won't protect you from the penalty.
Does the Part B late enrollment penalty ever go away?
No. Unlike the Part A penalty, which is temporary, the Part B penalty is permanent in nearly all cases. It's recalculated each year based on the current standard premium, but the percentage surcharge you owe stays with you for as long as you have Part B.
