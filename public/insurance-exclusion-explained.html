What Are Exclusions
in Insurance?

Policy Basics
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You filed a claim and your insurer denied it — not because you did anything wrong, but because the specific event that caused your loss isn't covered under your policy. That's an exclusion at work. Or maybe you're reading your policy for the first time and wondering why so many things seem to be carved out of coverage.

Every insurance policy has exclusions. They are the situations, events, and types of damage that your insurer explicitly will not pay for. They aren't hidden — they're written into your policy in black and white. The problem is most people never read that far, and they only discover an exclusion exists when a claim gets denied.

Understanding exclusions before you need to file a claim is one of the most valuable things you can do as a policyholder. It tells you exactly where your coverage ends — and where you may need to buy additional protection.

Quick answers

What people ask most

What are exclusions in insurance?
Exclusions are specific situations or types of damage your policy will not cover. Every policy has them. They define the outer boundary of what your insurer agreed to pay for when you signed up — everything outside that boundary is excluded.
What does excluded mean on insurance?
It means that specific loss or event will not be paid by your insurer, period. If you file a claim for something excluded, it will be denied. Common examples include flood damage on a standard homeowners policy or elective procedures on a health plan.
Can I get coverage for something excluded from my policy?
Often yes — through an endorsement, rider, or separate policy. Flood insurance, earthquake coverage, and scheduled jewelry coverage are all examples of add-ons that cover what standard policies exclude. You have to purchase them separately and proactively.
Where do I find exclusions in my policy?
Look for a section titled "Exclusions" or "What Is Not Covered" in your full policy document. Your declarations page won't list them in detail. If you can't find them, call your agent and ask them to walk you through the key exclusions for your specific plan.
Most common exclusions

What insurance typically won't cover

Exclusions vary by policy type, but certain categories show up across almost every kind of insurance. These are the ones that catch people off guard most often — especially when they assumed they were covered.

  • Flood damage — Standard homeowners and renters policies exclude flood. You need a separate flood insurance policy through the NFIP or a private carrier. This surprises more people than almost any other exclusion.
  • Earthquake damage — Also excluded from standard homeowners policies in most states. California residents in particular need a separate earthquake policy.
  • Intentional acts — If you intentionally cause damage or injury, your policy will not cover it. This applies across home, auto, and liability policies.
  • Normal wear and tear — Insurance covers sudden, accidental losses — not the gradual breakdown of your property over time. A roof that slowly deteriorates over 20 years is not a covered claim.
  • Business activity at home — Running a business from your home may not be covered under your standard homeowners policy. A home business endorsement or separate commercial policy is usually required.
  • Pre-existing conditions — Health insurance exclusions for pre-existing conditions were largely eliminated by the ACA for marketplace plans, but short-term health plans may still exclude them.
  • Cosmetic or elective procedures — Health plans exclude treatments deemed not medically necessary. Read our guide on what "not medically necessary" means if your claim was denied for this reason.
  • Negligent maintenance — If damage results from your failure to maintain your property, your insurer may deny the claim under a neglect or maintenance exclusion.
By policy type

Exclusions differ by what you're insuring

The exclusions in your policy depend heavily on the type of coverage you have. Here's how they break down across the most common policy types.

Home & Renters

Flood, earthquake, mold, sewer backup, and business equipment used for work are commonly excluded. Damage from pests like termites or rodents is almost always excluded as a maintenance issue.

Auto Insurance

Mechanical breakdown, wear and tear, and rideshare driving (Uber, Lyft) are common auto exclusions. Using your personal vehicle for commercial delivery may also void coverage during that use.

Health Insurance

Cosmetic surgery, experimental treatments, vision and dental (on most medical plans), and out-of-network care beyond emergency situations are frequently excluded from standard health plans.

Life Insurance

Suicide within the first two years of the policy, death during the commission of a crime, and in some policies, death from high-risk activities like skydiving may be excluded from the death benefit.

⚠️ Exclusions are not the same as coverage limits

A coverage limit caps how much your insurer will pay for a covered loss. An exclusion means they won't pay anything for that type of loss at all. These are two very different problems — and require two different solutions.

What you can do about it

How to close the gaps in your coverage

Finding out you have an exclusion doesn't mean you're stuck. In most cases you have options — you just have to act before a loss occurs, not after.

  1. Read your policy's exclusions section now. Don't wait for a claim denial to discover what isn't covered. Pull up your full policy document — not just the declarations page — and read the exclusions section front to back.
  2. Ask your agent to explain every exclusion that applies to you. A good agent will walk you through each one and tell you whether it's a real risk for your situation.
  3. Check if an endorsement can fill the gap. Many exclusions can be removed or covered by adding an endorsement to your existing policy. Sewer backup, home business equipment, and scheduled personal property are common add-ons.
  4. Purchase a separate policy for major excluded risks. Flood and earthquake are the two biggest. If you live in a flood zone or earthquake-prone area and don't have separate coverage, you are uninsured for those events.
  5. Review exclusions every year at renewal. Your situation changes. If you started a side business, added a pool, or bought expensive jewelry, your existing exclusions may now expose you to significant risk.
  6. If a claim was denied due to an exclusion, read it carefully. Insurers sometimes misapply exclusions. If you believe the exclusion doesn't actually apply to your situation, you have the right to appeal the denial.
What to do right now
Pull up your current policy documents today and find the exclusions section — not the summary, the full policy. Make a list of anything that surprises you and bring it to your agent at your next renewal. If your claim was just denied because of an exclusion, read the exact policy language carefully before accepting the denial as final — insurers sometimes misapply exclusions, and you have the right to appeal. And if you live in a flood-prone area without separate flood insurance, that is the single most urgent gap to close.
