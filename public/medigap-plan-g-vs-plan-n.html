Medigap Plan G vs Plan N
Which One Actually Costs Less?

Medicare & Medigap

Written by the InsurePlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

Plan G and Plan N are the two most popular Medigap (Medicare Supplement) plans sold today, largely because Plan F — which used to cover everything — is no longer available to people who became Medicare-eligible on or after January 1, 2020. That's pushed millions of shoppers into comparing G and N, and the decision usually comes down to one question: pay more every month for fewer surprises, or pay less every month and handle small costs as they come up.

Both plans cover the big-ticket items — hospital coinsurance, the Part A deductible, skilled nursing coinsurance, and 20% of Part B costs that Original Medicare doesn't pay. Where they split is in two places: excess charges and copays. That's a narrower gap than it sounds, but it can add up to a few hundred dollars a year depending on your doctors and how often you use them.

There's no universally "better" plan here — it's a math problem specific to your ZIP code, your carrier's pricing, and your health habits, and we'll walk through how to run that math yourself.

Quick answers

What people ask most

Is Plan N cheaper than Plan G?
Almost always, on the monthly premium. Plan N typically runs $20–$50 less per month. Whether it's cheaper overall depends on your copays and any excess charges you might hit.
What's the catch with Plan N's lower price?
Copays and excess charge exposure. Plan N charges up to $20 for office visits and up to $50 for ER visits that don't result in admission. It also doesn't cover Part B excess charges, unlike Plan G.
Which plan do most new Medicare enrollees pick?
Plan G is currently the most-sold Medigap plan nationally, largely because it's the closest replacement to the now-closed Plan F. Plan N is the fastest-growing alternative as people shop for lower premiums.
Coverage breakdown

What each plan actually covers

Both plans start from the same base of Medigap benefits, then diverge on two specific items. Here's the full picture, item by item:

  • Part A coinsurance & hospital costs — covered 100% by both plans, including an extra 365 days after Medicare benefits end.
  • Part B coinsurance (the 20%) — covered 100% by both plans.
  • Blood (first 3 pints) — covered 100% by both plans.
  • Skilled nursing facility coinsurance — covered 100% by both plans.
  • Part A deductible — covered 100% by both plans.
  • Part B excess charges — covered by Plan G only. Plan N leaves you responsible for this.
  • Foreign travel emergency — covered at 80% by both plans, up to plan limits.
  • Part B deductible — not covered by either plan (this changed when Plan F was phased out). You'll want to understand your out-of-pocket maximum before enrolling.
Head-to-head

Plan G vs Plan N, side by side

Here's the practical difference in how each plan feels to actually use, based on the real search phrasing people use when comparing plan g vs plan n cost and pros and cons.

Plan G

Higher monthly premium. No copays for doctor visits. Covers Part B excess charges. Best for people who see doctors often or live in a state where excess charges are common.

Plan N

Lower monthly premium ($20–$50/mo less on average). $20 copay for office visits, up to $50 ER copay. No excess charge coverage. Best for people who are generally healthy and want to save on the premium.

⚠️ Watch for excess charges

Only a handful of states restrict Part B excess charges outright. Everywhere else, a doctor who doesn't accept Medicare assignment can legally bill up to 15% above the Medicare-approved amount — and with Plan N, that bill is yours. Ask any specialist you see regularly whether they accept assignment before choosing Plan N.

How to decide

Working out which plan actually saves you money

  1. Get quotes for both plans from the same carrier in your ZIP code — premiums vary a lot by location and company, more than the coverage differences do.
  2. Estimate your annual doctor visits. Multiply expected office visits by the $20 Plan N copay to estimate your yearly copay exposure.
  3. Ask your regular doctors if they accept Medicare assignment. If they don't, factor in potential excess charges when comparing to Plan G.
  4. Compare the annual premium difference to your estimated Plan N costs. If the premium savings outweighs your copays and excess charge risk, Plan N likely wins.
  5. Factor in plan switching rules. If you're moving between plans, review how Medigap underwriting works, since switching later may require medical underwriting outside your initial enrollment window.
  6. Check your enrollment timing. If you're outside your Medigap open enrollment period, insurers can ask health questions — see what happens if you've missed your open enrollment window.
What to do right now
Request quotes for both Plan G and Plan N from two or three carriers in your area, then run the math above using your actual doctor visit history from the past year. If you're currently on Medicare Advantage and considering a move to Medigap, read up on switching from Medicare Advantage to Medigap before you apply, since timing affects whether you'll need to answer health questions.
Frequently asked questions
Is Plan N cheaper than Plan G?
Usually yes, on the monthly premium. Plan N typically costs $20 to $50 less per month than Plan G. But Plan N adds copays and doesn't cover Part B excess charges, so your total annual cost depends on how often you see doctors.
What is the main difference between Plan G and Plan N?
Plan G covers Part B excess charges and has no copays for doctor visits. Plan N has a lower premium but requires small copays ($20 for office visits, up to $50 for ER visits) and doesn't cover excess charges.
What are Part B excess charges?
Excess charges happen when a doctor doesn't accept Medicare assignment and can legally bill up to 15% more than the Medicare-approved amount. Plan G covers this extra cost; Plan N does not, leaving you responsible for it.
Does Plan N cover everything Plan G covers?
No. Plan N covers the same core benefits as Plan G except for the Part B deductible (neither covers this) and Part B excess charges (only Plan G covers this). Plan N also adds copays that Plan G doesn't have.
Which is better for someone who rarely visits the doctor?
Plan N often works out cheaper overall for people with few doctor visits, since the lower premium outweighs the occasional copay. Frequent visitors or those in states with common excess charges may save more with Plan G.
