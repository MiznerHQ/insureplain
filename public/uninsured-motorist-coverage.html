What Is Uninsured
Motorist Coverage?

Car Insurance
⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

Uninsured motorist coverage (UM) is the part of your auto insurance policy that protects you when the driver who hits you has no insurance at all. Instead of trying to collect from a driver who can't pay, you file with your own insurer — and they pay you.

About 1 in 8 drivers on U.S. roads carries zero insurance. UM coverage exists specifically for that scenario. Without it, being hit by an uninsured driver can leave you paying your own medical bills and repair costs out of pocket.

The two types of UM coverage

What it actually covers

UMBI — Bodily Injury

Covers your medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering when an uninsured driver injures you or your passengers. This is the most important type.

UMPD — Property Damage

Covers damage to your vehicle when an uninsured driver hits you. Some states require a small deductible ($200–$300). Not available in all states.

📋 Underinsured Motorist (UIM) — the close cousin

UIM coverage kicks in when the other driver has insurance but not enough to cover your full damages. If they carry $25,000 in liability but your bills are $80,000, UIM pays the gap. Often sold alongside UM as a package.

What UM coverage pays for

Specific scenarios it covers

  • Hit by an uninsured driver — The core use case. Your UM pays your medical bills and lost wages directly.
  • Hit-and-run accidents — In most states, UM covers hit-and-run situations where the at-fault driver fled and can't be identified.
  • Passenger injuries — Your passengers are typically covered under your UMBI if they're injured in your vehicle.
  • Pedestrian or cyclist accidents — Many policies extend UMBI coverage if you're hit by an uninsured driver while walking or cycling.
  • Vehicle damage — UMPD covers your car repairs when an uninsured driver is at fault (where available).
Is it required?

States where UM is mandatory

Uninsured motorist coverage is required by law in about half of U.S. states. In other states it's optional but must be offered by your insurer — you have to actively decline it in writing if you don't want it.

Required States (examples)

New York, Illinois, Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, Connecticut, Kansas, Oregon — and others. Check your state's DMV or insurance commissioner website for your state's exact rules.

Optional But Offered

In states like California, Texas, and Florida, UM is optional. Your insurer must offer it but you can decline. Declining in a state with high uninsured driver rates is a significant risk.

Do I need it?

When UM coverage is worth it

The honest answer: almost always yes. Here's why the math works in your favor.

  • It's cheap — UM coverage typically adds $5–$20 per month to your premium depending on your state and limits. That's one of the best value-per-dollar coverages available.
  • 1 in 8 drivers is uninsured — The odds of encountering an uninsured driver in your driving lifetime are significant.
  • Medical bills are expensive — A single ER visit after an accident can run $10,000–$30,000 or more. UMBI absorbs that cost.
  • Health insurance gaps exist — Even with health insurance, you may face deductibles, copays, and no coverage for lost wages. UMBI fills those gaps.
  • Hit-and-run protection — Collision covers your car in a hit-and-run but not your medical bills. UMBI does.
If you're choosing between adding UM or increasing your collision limits, UM is almost always the better call — especially for bodily injury protection.
Frequently asked questions
Do I need UM coverage if I have health insurance?
Health insurance covers medical bills but not lost wages, pain and suffering, or your deductibles and copays. UMBI covers all of those. They complement each other rather than overlap — having health insurance doesn't make UM redundant.
Do I need UM coverage if I have full coverage?
"Full coverage" usually means liability + collision + comprehensive. Collision covers your vehicle damage regardless of fault. But collision does not cover your medical bills or lost wages — that's what UMBI does. So yes, UM adds protection that full coverage doesn't include.
What limits should I choose for UM coverage?
A common recommendation is to match your UM limits to your liability limits. If you carry 100/300 liability ($100,000 per person / $300,000 per accident), carry the same in UM. The higher the limit, the more protection you have — and the cost difference is usually small.
Does UM cover a hit-and-run?
In most states, yes — UMBI covers hit-and-run accidents where the driver fled and cannot be identified. Some states require physical contact with the other vehicle to qualify. Check your policy or ask your insurer about your state's specific rules.
