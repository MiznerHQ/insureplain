What Is Disability Insurance
and How Much Does It Cost?

Disability Insurance

Written by the InsurePlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. InsurePlain does not provide insurance, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified insurance professional about your specific situation.
What's actually happening

You've probably got life insurance figured out, and maybe health insurance too. But there's a bigger financial risk most people never insure against: losing their paycheck because they get sick or hurt and can't work. That's what disability insurance is for.

It's not a niche product. Roughly one in four of today's 20-year-olds can expect to be out of work for at least a year due to a disabling condition before they reach retirement age, yet a large share of working adults have no coverage beyond the bare minimum Social Security provides. Understanding what this coverage actually does — and what it costs — is the first step to deciding whether you're exposed.

Quick answers

What people ask most

WHAT DOES DISABILITY INSURANCE ACTUALLY PAY FOR?
It replaces a portion of your income, not your medical bills. If an illness or injury keeps you from working, the policy pays you a monthly benefit — typically 40-80% of your prior income — so you can keep covering rent, groceries, and other expenses while you're out.
IS DISABILITY INSURANCE WORTH IT FOR SELF-EMPLOYED PEOPLE?
Often more so than for employees. Self-employed workers have no employer group plan as a backstop, so an individual policy may be the only source of income replacement available if they can't work.
DOES DISABILITY INSURANCE COVER PREGNANCY?
Short-term disability policies commonly do. Recovery from childbirth is one of the most common reasons short-term disability benefits get paid out, though coverage terms vary by state and by policy.
The basics

Short-term vs. long-term disability

Disability insurance comes in two main flavors, and most people benefit from understanding both even if they only end up buying one.

Short-term disability

Pays a portion of income — often around 60% — for a few weeks up to roughly six months. Common uses: surgery recovery, childbirth, a serious short-term illness.

Long-term disability

Pays roughly 50-80% of income for years, sometimes until retirement age, after a waiting period of several months. Used for serious, recurring, or permanent conditions.

⚠️ THE WAITING PERIOD MATTERS

Long-term policies don't start paying immediately — most have an elimination period of 90 to 180 days after you become disabled. Some people pair short-term coverage with long-term coverage specifically to bridge that gap.

Pricing

What disability insurance actually costs

Cost is the single most-searched part of this topic right now, and for good reason — the range is wide depending on how you get your coverage.

  • Individual long-term policy — generally runs about 1-3% of your annual salary per year. On a $75,000 salary that's roughly $60-$190 a month; on a $200,000 salary it can run $170-$500 a month.
  • Employer-sponsored group plan — usually far cheaper for the employee, often in the $10-$60 a month range, since the employer covers part or all of the premium.
  • Age at purchase — like most insurance, buying younger and healthier locks in a lower rate for the life of the policy.
  • Occupation class — higher-risk or physically demanding jobs generally cost more to insure than desk jobs.
  • Gender — many insurers price policies by sex, and women are commonly quoted around 15% higher premiums than men for the same coverage.
  • Elimination period and non-cancelable riders — choosing a longer waiting period before benefits start can lower your premium, similar to how a higher deductible lowers premiums on other policies. Adding a non-cancelable guarantee (the insurer can't raise your rate or cancel the policy) typically adds 10-20% to the cost.
The coverage gap

Why employer coverage often isn't enough

If you have disability coverage at all, it's most likely through work — but group plans have real limits worth knowing about.

  • Partial income replacement — group long-term disability commonly caps around 60-65% of base salary, often with a dollar cap per month regardless of how high your salary is.
  • Taxable benefits — if your employer paid the premiums, the benefit you receive is generally taxable, which shrinks your real take-home replacement further.
  • Coverage ends when you leave the job — unlike an individual policy, group disability coverage typically doesn't follow you if you change employers.
  • No coverage for bonuses or equity — group plans usually calculate benefits off base salary only, which matters for anyone with variable compensation. This is a similar gap to what shows up with rising premium costs squeezing take-home pay elsewhere, or an out-of-pocket maximum that resets each year.

This is why many higher earners, business owners, and self-employed workers layer an individual policy on top of — or instead of — group coverage.

The public safety net

How SSDI compares

Social Security Disability Insurance exists, but it was never designed to fully replace a paycheck.

SSDI (government)

Strict medical definition of disability. Waiting period of about five months before benefits start. Average monthly benefit is roughly $1,630, well below most people's actual income needs.

Private disability insurance

Generally easier to qualify for after a claim, pays a much higher share of income, and can start collecting sooner than SSDI typically does.

What to do right now
Check whether you have any disability coverage through work today, and if so, what percentage of your salary it replaces and whether it's capped. If you're self-employed, have no employer plan, or earn above your group plan's cap, get quotes for an individual policy — insurers will typically want recent tax returns or pay stubs to verify income. If you're planning ahead for later in life, our guide on long-term care insurance covers a related but separate type of protection worth understanding too.
Frequently asked questions
How much does disability insurance cost per month?
Individual long-term disability policies typically run about 1-3% of your annual salary per year, which works out to roughly $30-$100 a month for many buyers, though it can run several hundred dollars a month for higher earners. Employer-sponsored group plans are usually far cheaper, often $10-$60 a month, because the employer pays part or all of the premium. Your actual rate depends on your age, health, occupation, and how much coverage you choose.
What's the difference between short-term and long-term disability insurance?
Short-term disability insurance generally pays a portion of your income for a few weeks up to about six months, and is often used for recovery from surgery, childbirth, or a temporary illness. Long-term disability insurance kicks in after a longer waiting period and can pay benefits for years, sometimes until retirement age, for more serious or permanent conditions. Some policies are designed to work together, with long-term coverage starting right after short-term benefits end.
Does my employer's disability insurance cover enough?
Often not entirely. Employer group plans commonly replace somewhere in the range of 40-60% of your salary, and the benefit may be taxable if your employer paid the premiums, which shrinks the payout further. Many people supplement group coverage with an individual policy to close the gap, especially if they have a mortgage, dependents, or an income above the group plan's coverage cap.
How does Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) compare to private disability insurance?
SSDI is a federal safety net, not a replacement for private coverage. It has a strict definition of disability, a waiting period of several months before benefits begin, and an average monthly benefit far below what most people earn. Private disability insurance is generally easier to qualify for after a claim, pays a higher percentage of income, and starts collecting much sooner than SSDI typically does.
Can I get disability insurance if I'm self-employed?
Yes. Since self-employed workers usually have no employer group plan to fall back on, an individual disability policy is often the only income protection available if illness or injury stops you from working. Insurers will typically ask for proof of consistent income, such as tax returns, to determine how much coverage you can qualify for.
